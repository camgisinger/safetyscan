CHUNK 1: Confined Space — Definition and Classification
Section: WHS Regulation s5; COP s1.1
Topic: What is a confined space and how to determine whether a space qualifies

A "CONFINED SPACE" is an enclosed or partially enclosed space that:
(a) Is NOT DESIGNED OR INTENDED PRIMARILY TO BE OCCUPIED BY A PERSON
(b) Is, or is designed or intended to be, at NORMAL ATMOSPHERIC PRESSURE while any person is in the space
(c) Is, or is likely to be, a risk to health and safety from at least ONE of:
   - An atmosphere that DOES NOT HAVE A SAFE OXYGEN LEVEL
   - Contaminants (airborne gases, vapours, dusts) that may cause injury from FIRE OR EXPLOSION
   - HARMFUL CONCENTRATIONS of any airborne contaminants
   - ENGULFMENT

Does NOT include a mine shaft or the workings of a mine.

ENTRY into a confined space occurs when a person's HEAD OR UPPER BODY enters the space or crosses its boundary.

COMMON EXAMPLES of confined spaces (when they meet the definition):
- Vats, tanks, pits, pipes, ducts, flues, chimneys, silos
- Containers, pressure vessels
- Underground sewers, wet or dry wells
- Shafts, trenches (if atmospheric risk present), tunnels

WHAT IS NOT A CONFINED SPACE:
- Places intended for human occupancy with adequate ventilation, lighting and safe entry/exit (e.g. offices, workshops)
- Abrasive blasting or spray painting booths designed for human occupancy
- Spaces occasionally designed for occupancy with a READILY ACCESSIBLE DOOR AT GROUND LEVEL (e.g. cool stores with large ground-level openings, fumigated shipping containers with large ground-level openings)
- Trenches are NOT confined spaces based on structural collapse risk alone — they become confined spaces only if they may contain harmful airborne contaminant concentrations

DECLASSIFICATION RULE: A confined space CANNOT be declassified by temporary controls (e.g. temporary ventilation or a satisfactory pre-entry gas test). To be declassified, it must undergo permanent structural and use changes that eliminate ALL inherent hazards.

SAFE OXYGEN LEVEL: 19.5% to 23.5% by volume.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Tank, vessel, pit, silo, manhole, underground pipe access, or shaft = likely confined space — check criteria
- Space with restricted entry (ladder access, small hatch) = potential confined space
- Any space where a worker's head enters and which is not a normal human occupancy area = assess as confined space
- "Confined Space — Authorised Entry Only" sign = confirmed confined space on site
- Worker entering a manhole, tank, pit, or vessel = confined space entry requiring permit

CHUNK 2: Confined Space — Hazardous Atmospheres
Section: COP s3.2, s4.5; WHS Regulation s71, s72
Topic: Atmospheric hazards, oxygen levels, flammable limits, and contaminant thresholds

OXYGEN LEVELS:
- SAFE RANGE: 19.5% to 23.5% by volume
- OXYGEN DEFICIENCY (below 19.5%): causes impairment, loss of consciousness, asphyxiation
- OXYGEN ENRICHMENT (above 23.5%): significantly increases fire and explosion risk
- Air-supplied respiratory equipment MUST be provided to any worker entering a space with oxygen level LESS THAN 19.5% by volume

CAUSES OF OXYGEN DEPLETION:
- Biological processes (e.g. methane from sewers displacing oxygen)
- Purging with inert gas
- Surface oxidation/rusting inside metal tanks and vessels
- Combustion of flammable substances
- Absorption by grains, wood chips, soil, or chemicals in sealed silos

FLAMMABLE GAS LIMITS (LEL = Lower Explosive Limit):
- SAFE CONDITION: flammable gas/vapour/mist concentration LESS THAN 5% of LEL
- DANGER ZONE: 5% to <10% of LEL — worker must be removed UNLESS a suitably calibrated CONTINUOUS-MONITORING FLAMMABLE GAS DETECTOR is in use
- IMMEDIATE EVACUATION: concentration EQUAL TO OR GREATER THAN 10% of LEL — worker must be immediately removed
- A FLAMMABLE ATMOSPHERE is one where flammable gas, vapour or mist is likely to EXCEED 5% of its LEL

PURGING RULES:
- Purging is done using INERT GAS (e.g. nitrogen) to clear flammable gases before entry
- PROHIBITION: Pure oxygen or gas mixtures with oxygen concentration GREATER THAN 21% by volume MUST NOT be used for purging or ventilating
- After purging, ventilate with fresh air to restore oxygen to NORMAL LEVEL OF 21%
- Atmospheric testing must be done BEFORE ENTRY after purging

TESTING REQUIREMENTS (s71):
Testing must cover:
- OXYGEN CONTENT
- Airborne concentration of FLAMMABLE CONTAMINANTS
- Airborne concentration of POTENTIALLY HARMFUL CONTAMINANTS (e.g. H₂S, carbon monoxide)

Testing methods:
- INITIAL TESTING: from OUTSIDE the space using a sample probe inserted through access holes — test side to side AND top to bottom
- Hydrogen sulphide (H₂S): heavier than air — settles to BOTTOM
- Methane: lighter than air — collects at TOP
- Agitate liquids before monitoring as gases may be dissolved and released on disturbance
- NEVER use human senses to determine if air is safe — many toxic/flammable gases are odourless

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Gas detector being used before entry = positive compliance
- Worker entering space without atmospheric test = serious non-compliance
- LEL reading at 5% or above = immediate concern — check for continuous monitoring or evacuate
- Oxygen reading below 19.5% or above 23.5% = non-compliant entry without supplied air RPE
- Purging with oxygen cylinders = dangerous and prohibited
- LPG/combustion engine operating near confined space opening = carbon monoxide risk, flag

CHUNK 3: Confined Space — Entry Permit Requirements
Section: WHS Regulation s65, s67, s77; COP s4.3
Topic: Mandatory entry permit content, process, and record-keeping

PROHIBITION: A PCBU MUST NOT allow or direct a worker to enter a confined space to carry out work UNLESS the worker has been issued a CONFINED SPACE ENTRY PERMIT.

Entry permit is also required when entering to conduct the INITIAL HAZARD IDENTIFICATION OR RISK ASSESSMENT.

THE PERMIT MUST be completed IN WRITING by a COMPETENT PERSON and must:
(a) SPECIFY the confined space to which it relates
(b) RECORD the NAMES OF ALL PERSONS permitted to enter and the PERIOD OF TIME work will be carried out
(c) SET OUT RISK CONTROL MEASURES based on the risk assessment, including:
   - Control measures for SAFE ENTRY
   - Details of SYSTEMS OF WORK
   - List of equipment to be taken into the space
   - Exclusions (e.g. ignition sources)
   - Emergency rescue equipment required
(d) Contain space for ACKNOWLEDGEMENT that work is completed and ALL PERSONS HAVE EXITED

PERMIT SCOPE:
- A single permit can cover MULTIPLE ENTRIES into one space and MULTIPLE ACCESS POINTS into the same single space
- ONE PERMIT = ONE CONFINED SPACE
- Permit must be RE-VALIDATED if: the person in direct management changes; a break in work continuity occurs; changes introduce new hazards; or new control measures are needed

RECORD RETENTION:
- Permit must be kept until work is COMPLETED
- If a NOTIFIABLE INCIDENT occurs: keep for AT LEAST 2 YEARS after work completion
- Risk assessment copy must be kept for AT LEAST 28 DAYS after work completion, or 2 years after a notifiable incident
- Risk assessment must be available to any relevant worker ON REQUEST
- TRAINING RECORDS must be kept for 2 YEARS

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Entry permit visible/posted at confined space entrance = positive compliance
- Worker entering confined space without visible permit = serious non-compliance
- Permit not listing names of persons inside = non-compliant
- Entry permit without atmospheric test results recorded = incomplete, flag
- Permit not displaying expiry time/date = flag

CHUNK 4: Confined Space — Risk Assessment Requirements
Section: WHS Regulation s66, s77; COP s3.4
Topic: Mandatory risk assessment obligations, content, and competency requirements

MANDATORY: A risk assessment MUST be conducted BEFORE confined space work. It MUST be:
- Conducted by a COMPETENT PERSON (person with training, qualification or experience)
- RECORDED IN WRITING
- REVIEWED AND REVISED whenever risks change

Risk assessment must consider at minimum:
(a) Whether work CAN BE CARRIED OUT WITHOUT ENTRY into the space
(b) The NATURE OF THE CONFINED SPACE
(c) CHANGES IN OXYGEN CONCENTRATION or airborne contaminant concentration
(d) The WORK METHOD and range of methods available
(e) The TYPE OF EMERGENCY PROCEDURES required (including rescue)

Additional factors to assess:
- Risk of ENGULFMENT
- Number of persons in the space
- Substances LAST CONTAINED in the space (indicates likely hazards)
- Whether the space needs to be brought to ATMOSPHERIC PRESSURE before entry
- Number of persons required OUTSIDE the space for communication, equipment, and emergency response
- Risks from noise, electricity, biological hazards, mechanical plant
- Physiological and psychological demands on workers

GENERIC RISK ASSESSMENTS: Acceptable only where hazards, tasks, and circumstances are IDENTICAL across all spaces covered. Individual risk assessment required if hazards, tasks, or circumstances DIFFER.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Written risk assessment available on site = positive
- No written risk assessment for confined space work = serious non-compliance
- Risk assessment not reviewed after change in work conditions = non-compliant
- Competency of person who conducted risk assessment not evident = flag

CHUNK 5: Confined Space — Isolation Requirements
Section: COP s4.4; AS 2865
Topic: Energy and service isolation before confined space entry

ALL POTENTIALLY HAZARDOUS PLANT AND SERVICES must be ISOLATED BEFORE ENTRY.

Isolation must prevent:
- Introduction of hazardous contaminants through piping, ducts, vents, drains, conveyors, service pipes
- ACTIVATION OR ENERGISING of machinery inside the space
- Activation of heating/refrigerating or other plant outside the space that could affect the space
- Release of STORED OR POTENTIAL ENERGY
- Inadvertent use of electrical equipment

PIPE WORK ISOLATION — acceptable methods (choose one):
1. REMOVE valve, spool piece or expansion joint; then BLANK OR CAP the open end of piping (cap/blank must be tagged)
2. INSERT a FULL-PRESSURE SPADE OR BLANK between flanges as close as practicable to the space (must be tagged)
3. CLOSE, LOCK AND TAG at least TWO VALVES in piping leading to the space, with a DRAIN OR VENT VALVE between the two closed valves LOCKED OPEN to atmosphere

MOVING PARTS AND STORED ENERGY:
- All machinery with agitators, fans, blades must be REDUCED TO ZERO ENERGY condition
- Energy types to address: hydraulic, pneumatic, electrical, chemical, mechanical, thermal
- Methods: chocking, wedging, chaining, or removing parts; lockout and tagout of equipment

LOCK AND TAG RULES:
- Key must be kept by the PERSON PLACING THE LOCK
- Spare keys must not be accessible except in emergencies
- Tag must indicate a PERSON IS IN THE SPACE and isolation must not be removed until all persons have exited

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Lockout/tagout padlocks and "DO NOT OPERATE" tags on valves/isolators = positive compliance
- Valve or switch to confined space without lockout/tagout = non-compliant
- Machinery inside confined space not de-energised before entry = serious non-compliance
- Blanks or spades visible and tagged on piping = positive isolation
- No isolation evidence for energised services to confined space = life-threatening non-compliance

CHUNK 6: Confined Space — Standby Person and Communication
Section: WHS Regulation s69; COP s4.6
Topic: Standby person duties and communication requirements

MANDATORY: A SYSTEM OF WORK must be in place that includes:
(a) CONTINUOUS COMMUNICATION with the worker from outside the confined space
(b) MONITORING OF CONDITIONS within the confined space by a STANDBY PERSON in the vicinity of the space

A STANDBY PERSON MUST BE ASSIGNED before any worker enters a confined space.

STANDBY PERSON MUST:
- REMAIN OUTSIDE the confined space at ALL TIMES
- DO NO OTHER WORK that may interfere with their primary monitoring role
- Understand the NATURE OF HAZARDS inside the space and recognise symptoms workers may experience
- Have ALL REQUIRED RESCUE EQUIPMENT IMMEDIATELY AVAILABLE (harnesses, lifting equipment, lifeline)
- Have AUTHORITY TO ORDER WORKERS TO EXIT if a hazardous situation arises
- NEVER ENTER THE SPACE TO ATTEMPT RESCUE (emergency services trained for rescue are to be used)
- Communicate with persons inside the space using voice, radio, hand signals or other suitable methods

COMMUNICATION SYSTEM must enable:
- Communication between persons inside and outside the space
- Summoning help in an emergency

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Person stationed outside confined space during entry = positive compliance (standby person)
- Worker in confined space with no one stationed outside = serious non-compliance
- Standby person performing other tasks while workers are inside = non-compliant
- Rescue equipment (harness, lifeline, hoist) visible at confined space opening = positive
- No communication system visible for workers inside space = flag
- "Confined Space — Authorised Entry Only" sign posted at entrance = positive

CHUNK 7: Confined Space — Signage and Barricades
Section: WHS Regulation s68; COP s4.8
Topic: Required signage and access control at confined space entry points

BEFORE any work in relation to a confined space starts, SIGNS MUST BE ERECTED at EACH ENTRANCE to the confined space.

Signs must:
- WARN AGAINST ENTRY by persons not listed on the confined space entry permit
- Be in place: while PREPARING to work in the space; DURING WORK; and while PACKING UP after completion

SIGNAGE ALONE IS NOT SUFFICIENT — SECURITY DEVICES (locks, fixed barriers) must also be installed to prevent unauthorised entry.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- "DANGER — CONFINED SPACE — AUTHORISED ENTRY ONLY" sign at access point = compliant
- Confined space access without any signage = non-compliant (direct regulatory offence under s68)
- Sign present but no barrier or lock = partially compliant, flag
- Sign missing from ONE OR MORE entry points while work is being carried out = non-compliant
- Traffic cones alone without signage at confined space opening = inadequate

CHUNK 8: Confined Space — Ventilation Requirements
Section: COP s4.5; WHS Regulation s71
Topic: Ventilation methods and requirements to maintain safe atmosphere

VENTILATION must be provided to ESTABLISH AND MAINTAIN a safe atmosphere for as long as anyone is in the confined space.

VENTILATION OPTIONS:
- Natural ventilation: only adequate if confined space has SUFFICIENT OPENINGS
- Mechanical ventilation: required in most cases

MECHANICAL VENTILATION EQUIPMENT MUST be:
- CONTINUOUSLY MONITORED to ensure it operates while the space is occupied
- Controls CLEARLY IDENTIFIED, TAGGED AND PROTECTED against unauthorised interference

TYPES OF MECHANICAL VENTILATION:
- LOCAL EXHAUST VENTILATION (LEV): effective where contaminant source is localised and extraction point can be close to the source (e.g. capturing welding fumes)
- DILUTION VENTILATION: air introduced to circulate throughout the space — supply point must ensure air is FRESH, BREATHABLE AND FREE OF CONTAMINANTS (must not source from near combustion engines or other pollutant sources)

PROHIBITION: Ventilation must NOT introduce oxygen at concentrations GREATER THAN 21% by volume.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Forced ventilation hose/duct running into confined space during occupied work = positive
- No ventilation in small confined space with workers inside = flag — assess oxygen levels
- Ventilation drawing supply air from near a running engine or exhaust = non-compliant
- Mechanical ventilation without monitoring = flag
- Oxygen or enriched-air cylinders being used for ventilation = prohibited

CHUNK 9: Confined Space — Respiratory Protective Equipment (RPE)
Section: COP s4.5; AS/NZS 1715
Topic: When RPE is required and what type must be used

AIR-SUPPLIED RESPIRATORY EQUIPMENT (RPE) MUST be provided when:
- The confined space CANNOT ACHIEVE A SAFE OXYGEN LEVEL (below 19.5% by volume)
- Safe levels of AIRBORNE CONTAMINANTS cannot be achieved
- There is NO EXPOSURE STANDARD for a substance, or the concentration is UNKNOWN
- The atmosphere is in any DOUBT

RPE TYPES:
- AIR-SUPPLIED respiratory equipment (airline breathing apparatus): preferred for most confined space work
- SELF-CONTAINED BREATHING APPARATUS (SCBA): for rescue and situations where airline supply is impractical

CONSERVATIVE APPROACH: When in any doubt about the airborne contaminant or RPE type required, use AIR-SUPPLIED RESPIRATORY EQUIPMENT.

RESCUE ENTRY: Workers performing rescue who enter a confined space in an emergency must be provided with AIR-SUPPLIED RPE if:
- The atmosphere DOES NOT HAVE A SAFE OXYGEN LEVEL; OR
- There is a HARMFUL CONCENTRATION of an airborne contaminant; OR
- There is SERIOUS RISK the atmosphere may become unsafe while the rescuer is inside

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Airline breathing apparatus or SCBA visible at confined space entry = positive (RPE provided)
- Worker entering confined space with only a dust mask or half-face respirator where oxygen deficiency or unknown contaminants exist = serious non-compliance
- No RPE available at confined space where atmosphere is not verified safe = non-compliant
- Rescuer entering confined space without supplied-air RPE after a worker has been overcome = life-threatening non-compliance

CHUNK 10: Confined Space — Emergency Procedures
Section: WHS Regulation s74; COP Chapter 5
Topic: Emergency and rescue procedure requirements for confined spaces

MANDATORY: A PCBU must ESTABLISH and PRACTISE first aid and rescue procedures for confined space emergencies.

Emergency procedures must be initiated from OUTSIDE THE CONFINED SPACE as soon as practicable.

PCBU MUST ENSURE:
- Entry and exit openings are of SUFFICIENT SIZE to allow emergency access
- Openings are NOT OBSTRUCTED during work
- All plant, equipment and PPE provided for emergency rescue is MAINTAINED IN GOOD WORKING ORDER

FACTORS THAT MUST BE CONSIDERED when establishing emergency procedures:
- Geographic LOCATION of the confined space and distance from medical facilities
- How workers inside will COMMUNICATE in an emergency and how the alarm is raised
- Whether emergency personnel can access the site during NIGHT SHIFT, WEEKENDS AND HOLIDAYS
- The types of emergencies contemplated and appropriate RESCUE AND RESUSCITATION EQUIPMENT
- Whether rescue equipment is kept IN CLOSE PROXIMITY to the space for IMMEDIATE USE
- Whether rescuers are TRAINED, SUFFICIENTLY FIT, and CAPABLE of using rescue equipment (breathing apparatus, lifelines, firefighting equipment)
- Availability of first aid, trained first aiders, and local emergency services

RESCUE PRINCIPLE: Rescue should be performed from OUTSIDE the confined space where possible.

KEY RULE: If a person inside a confined space has been OVERCOME BY LACK OF OXYGEN or by airborne contaminants, ALWAYS ASSUME that entry for rescue is UNSAFE unless AIR-SUPPLIED RESPIRATORY EQUIPMENT is used.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Emergency rescue equipment (harness, tripod hoist, lifeline) staged at confined space opening = positive
- Emergency procedures document visible at work area = positive
- No rescue equipment at confined space entry = non-compliant
- Confined space opening too small for rescue access = flag — design issue, must be addressed
- Stand-by person without rescue equipment = non-compliant
- Local emergency services not notified of confined space work at remote site = flag

CHUNK 11: Confined Space — Training Requirements
Section: WHS Regulation s76; COP s1.4
Topic: Who must be trained and what training must cover

RELEVANT WORKERS who must receive training include:
- Workers who ENTER OR WORK IN a confined space
- Workers who UNDERTAKE HAZARD IDENTIFICATION or risk assessment
- Workers who IMPLEMENT RISK CONTROL MEASURES
- Workers who COMPLETE ENTRY PERMITS
- STANDBY PERSONS and those who MONITOR CONDITIONS
- Workers who PURCHASE EQUIPMENT for confined space work
- SUPERVISORS of any of the above

TRAINING MUST COVER:
(a) NATURE OF ALL HAZARDS associated with the confined space
(b) NEED FOR AND APPROPRIATE USE of risk control measures
(c) Selection, fit, use, wearing, testing, storage and maintenance of ALL PPE required
(d) CONTENTS OF THE CONFINED SPACE ENTRY PERMIT
(e) EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

TRAINING RECORD RETENTION: Training records must be kept for AT LEAST 2 YEARS.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers unable to explain permit requirements or emergency procedures = inadequate training, flag
- Standby person unaware of hazards inside the space = non-compliant training
- No training records available for confined space workers = non-compliant
- New workers on site performing confined space duties without induction = flag

CHUNK 12: Confined Space — Hazard Types Reference
Section: COP s3.2, s3.3
Topic: Full list of hazards to identify and assess in confined spaces

ATMOSPHERIC HAZARDS:
- Oxygen deficiency (below 19.5% — asphyxiation risk)
- Oxygen enrichment (above 23.5% — fire and explosion risk)
- Toxic gases: hydrogen sulphide (H₂S), carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO₂)
- Flammable gases: methane, petroleum vapours, solvent vapours
- Airborne dust: flour, grain, coal, ceramic dust
- Residues in tanks/vessels that evaporate on disturbance

ENGULFMENT HAZARDS:
- Grain, sand, fly ash, fertiliser, animal feed, coal, sewage, liquid
- "BRIDGING" — grain/material forms a crust over a void; workers on the crust or below it risk engulfment if the bridge collapses

BIOLOGICAL HAZARDS:
- Sewers, grain silos, manure pits — viruses, bacteria, fungi causing disease or dermatitis

MECHANICAL HAZARDS:
- Augers, agitators, blenders, mixers, stirrers — entanglement, crushing, cutting

ELECTRICAL HAZARDS:
- Cables, transformers, capacitors, relays, exposed terminals, wet surfaces

NOISE HAZARDS:
- Amplified by hard surfaces inside confined spaces — may prevent hearing warning signals

ENVIRONMENTAL HAZARDS:
- Heat or cold stress from work, process or conditions
- Slippery surfaces, poor lighting
- Vertical openings — fall-in risk for persons outside

EXTERNAL SOURCES OF CONTAMINATION:
- COMBUSTION ENGINES operating near opening — carbon monoxide build-up inside space
- Hot work near adjacent confined space containing flammable atmosphere — explosion risk
- Traffic hazards at confined space entries on footpaths or roads

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Evidence of previous contents (residue, staining, pipe connections) in tank/vessel = assess for atmospheric hazard
- Silo, grain bin, or slurry tank = engulfment risk
- Workers in sewer or drain pit = H₂S/methane risk — test before entry
- Running engine or generator near confined space opening = carbon monoxide risk, flag
- Agitator or mixer present in vessel = mechanical isolation required before entry

CHUNK 13: Confined Space — Elimination and Minimisation (Hierarchy)
Section: COP s4.1, s4.2; WHS Regulation s66
Topic: Hierarchy of control — eliminating the need to enter confined spaces

FIRST PRIORITY — ELIMINATE THE NEED TO ENTER:
Work should always be carried out from outside the confined space where practicable. Examples:
- HIGH-PRESSURE HOSES inserted through access hatches to clean tanks
- REMOTE CAMERAS or mirrors on probes for internal inspection
- REMOTELY OPERATED FLAIL DEVICES or air purgers to clear silo blockages
- HOOKS, LONG-HANDLED CLASPS or magnets on strings to retrieve dropped objects

IF ENTRY CANNOT BE AVOIDED — apply hierarchy in order:
1. SUBSTITUTION: change work methods to minimise time inside or number of entrants
2. ISOLATION: isolate moving parts, electrical services, pipework
3. ENGINEERING CONTROLS: forced extraction ventilation, purging
4. ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS: training, entry permits, standby persons, communication systems
5. PPE (LAST RESORT): RPE, harnesses, hearing protection, protective clothing

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Evidence of remote inspection tools being used before sending workers in = positive best practice
- Workers entering confined space to perform tasks that could be done from outside = flag, not best practice
- Only PPE and administrative controls used (no engineering controls) where engineering is practicable = non-compliant hierarchy application
