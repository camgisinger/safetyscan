CHUNK 1: Definition and Scope — What is a Mobile Crane?
Section: CoP s1.1; WHS Regulation s219
Topic: Legal definitions of mobile crane types, vehicle-loading cranes and other mobile plant used as cranes

MOBILE CRANE means a crane capable of travelling over a supporting surface without the need for fixed runways, relying only on gravity for stability.

FOUR GENERAL TYPES of mobile crane in Australia:
1. HYDRAULIC SLEWING CRANES — boom/jib can slew; most common type
2. LATTICE BOOM CRANES (including crawler cranes)
3. NON-SLEWING (PICK-AND-CARRY) CRANES — boom/jib cannot slew; includes articulated and locomotive cranes
4. VEHICLE-LOADING CRANES — crane mounted on a vehicle for loading/unloading

SLEWING MOBILE CRANE — incorporates a boom or jib that can slew; does NOT include earthmoving equipment (front-end loaders, backhoes, excavators in crane mode).

NON-SLEWING MOBILE CRANE (pick-and-carry) — includes:
- Articulated mobile cranes
- Locomotive cranes
NOTE: A vehicle tow truck is NOT a non-slewing mobile crane.

VEHICLE-LOADING CRANE — a crane mounted on a vehicle for the purpose of loading and unloading that vehicle.

OTHER MOBILE PLANT — machines primarily used for activities other than lifting/lowering freely suspended loads; includes backhoes, excavators, front-end loaders, and telescopic handlers.

SCOPE: This CoP covers mobile cranes, vehicle-loading cranes, and other mobile plant used as mobile cranes to raise or lower a freely suspended load.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Any crane travelling over the ground without fixed runways = mobile crane subject to this CoP
- Excavator or backhoe with suspended load attached = other mobile plant used as crane; additional requirements apply
- Non-slewing crane operating with a suspended load while moving = pick-and-carry mode; specific stability rules apply
- Vehicle with crane mounted for loading/unloading = vehicle-loading crane; specific requirements in Section 14

CHUNK 2: Duty Holders — Who Has Health and Safety Responsibilities?
Section: CoP s1.2; WHS Act s19, s22–s29, s46, s47; WHS Regulation s34–s38, s219, s243–s245
Topic: Legal duties of PCBUs, principal contractors, crane owners, operators, doggers, officers, workers and others

PRIMARY DUTY — PCBU (WHS Act s19):
- Must ELIMINATE health and safety risks from mobile crane operations
- If elimination not reasonably practicable: MINIMISE risks so far as is reasonably practicable
- Must ensure provision and maintenance of safe plant (including mobile cranes)
- Must ensure safe use, handling, storage and transport of plant

CRANE OWNER as PCBU — management or control of plant:
- If you own a mobile crane, you are the person with management or control of that plant
- Crane owner hiring/leasing plant to others has duties as both supplier and person with management or control
- Must ensure plant is safe to use and properly maintained
- Must provide specific information about how to operate the plant safely

HIRING ARRANGEMENTS:
- Both the person hiring AND the person supplying the plant must ensure the plant is safe, so far as reasonably practicable
- During hire, the hirer has control over how the plant is used
- For extended hire: parties may develop arrangements for inspection and maintenance; hirer should provide all maintenance records to crane owner at end of hire

DESIGNERS, MANUFACTURERS, IMPORTERS, SUPPLIERS (WHS Act s22–s26):
- Must ensure plant is without risks to health and safety
- Must carry out analysis, testing or examination and provide specific information
- Information must pass from designer through to end user
- Suppliers must provide design registration number when supplying a crane requiring plant design registration

OFFICERS (WHS Act s27):
- Must exercise DUE DILIGENCE
- Must take reasonable steps to ensure business has appropriate resources and processes to eliminate or minimise plant risks

WORKERS AND OTHER PERSONS (WHS Act s28):
- Must take reasonable care for own health and safety
- Must co-operate with reasonable policies, procedures and instructions
- Must not adversely affect other people's health and safety

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane owner cannot demonstrate inspection and maintenance records = flag
- Plant in use without documented handover of safety information from supplier = non-compliance
- No design registration number available for crane with >10t rated capacity = non-compliance
- Workers operating without instruction on crane-specific hazards = flag

CHUNK 3: Risk Management — Identifying and Controlling Mobile Crane Risks
Section: CoP s1.3; WHS Regulation s34, s35, s36, s37, s38
Topic: Four-step risk management process; hazard identification; hierarchy of controls

RISK MANAGEMENT PROCESS — four steps:
1. IDENTIFY HAZARDS — observe workplace, ask crane operator/crew, review inspection and maintenance records including logbooks, incident and injury records (including near misses)
2. ASSESS RISKS — understand nature of harm, severity, and likelihood
3. IMPLEMENT CONTROL MEASURES — using hierarchy of controls
4. MONITOR AND REVIEW — regularly review effectiveness; consider changes, nature and duration of work

KEY HAZARDS IN MOBILE CRANE OPERATIONS:
- Structural failure (boom, jib, hydraulic rams, wire rope)
- Crane overturning
- Contact or collision of crane or load with people, plant or structures
- Falling objects

HIERARCHY OF CONTROL MEASURES (WHS Regulation s36) — from highest to lowest protection:
1. ELIMINATION — remove the hazard
2. SUBSTITUTION — replace hazard with lesser risk
3. ISOLATION — separate hazard from person (e.g. exclusion zones)
4. ENGINEERING CONTROLS — physical measures (e.g. rated capacity limiters, motion limiting devices)
5. ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS — procedures, SWMS, documented lifting procedures
6. PPE — last resort; must not be sole control

CONSULTATION REQUIREMENTS (WHS Act s47):
- Must consult workers who are, or are likely to be, directly affected by a matter relating to WHS
- If workers are represented by a health and safety representative, consultation must involve that representative

COORDINATION (WHS Act s46):
- Where more than one person has a duty regarding the same matter, each must consult, co-operate and co-ordinate with all others

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No documented risk assessment or SWMS for complex or high-risk lifts = non-compliance
- PPE used as sole control without higher-order controls = non-compliant approach
- No review of control measures after near miss or changed conditions = flag
- No evidence of worker consultation in planning of crane operations = flag

CHUNK 4: Safe Work Method Statements (SWMS) — When Required for Crane Operations
Section: CoP s1.4; WHS Regulation s299, s300
Topic: SWMS requirements for high risk construction work involving mobile cranes

SWMS REQUIRED for HIGH RISK CONSTRUCTION WORK — must be prepared BEFORE work starts.

HIGH RISK CONSTRUCTION WORK relevant to mobile crane operations includes:
- Work involving risk of a person falling more than 2 METRES
- Demolition of an element that is load-bearing or related to physical integrity of a structure
- Work involving or likely to involve disturbance of ASBESTOS
- Structural alterations or repairs requiring temporary support to prevent collapse
- Work in or near a CONFINED SPACE
- Work in or near a shaft or trench with EXCAVATED DEPTH GREATER THAN 1.5 METRES or a tunnel
- Work on or near ENERGISED ELECTRICAL INSTALLATIONS
- Work in areas with contaminated or flammable atmosphere
- Work involving TILT-UP OR PRECAST CONCRETE
- Work on, in or adjacent to a ROAD, RAILWAY, SHIPPING LANE or other traffic corridor in use by traffic or pedestrians
- Work at a workplace with MOVEMENT OF POWERED MOBILE PLANT
- Work near or above WATER (drowning risk)

COMPLEX LIFT TYPES ALWAYS REQUIRING COMPREHENSIVE SITE-SPECIFIC SWMS:
- Tilt-up and pre-cast concrete panel lifts
- Multiple crane lifts
- Lifting of workboxes with personnel
- Installation of bridge beams
- Working near live overhead powerlines
- Lifting large pressure vessels or tanks
- Cranes used on barges
- Mobile plant retrieval following an incident
- Erection of tower cranes
- Heavy lifts: bridge beams (10 TONNES OR MORE) or loads of 50 TONNES OR MORE

SWMS CONTENT — must:
- Identify the type of high-risk construction work being done
- Specify health and safety hazards and risks
- Describe how risks will be controlled
- Describe how control measures will be implemented, monitored and reviewed

SWMS DEVELOPMENT:
- Must be developed in CONSULTATION WITH WORKERS and their representatives
- May be incorporated into documented lifting procedures (and vice versa) to reduce duplication
- Generic SWMS may be used for same or very similar circumstances to past work
- Site-specific SWMS must be prepared for unique or complex lifts

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Complex or high-risk crane lift underway without SWMS available on site = serious non-compliance
- SWMS not reviewed after deviation, incident or near miss = non-compliance
- No evidence of worker consultation in SWMS development = flag
- Generic SWMS used for unique or complex lift type = flag

CHUNK 5: Design Registration and Plant Registration of Mobile Cranes
Section: CoP s2; WHS Regulation s243, s244, s245
Topic: Design registration requirements; when registration applies; what triggers re-registration

DESIGN REGISTRATION REQUIRED for mobile cranes with RATED CAPACITY GREATER THAN 10 TONNES (WHS Regulation s243, Schedule 5 Part 1).

ALTERED DESIGNS must be registered if the alteration MAY AFFECT HEALTH OR SAFETY (WHS Regulation s244).

Registration NOT required if design is already registered under a corresponding WHS law (WHS Regulation s245).

APPLICATION FOR DESIGN REGISTRATION must include:
- Statement signed by designer confirming compliance with WHS Act s22 and listing technical standards used
- Design verification statement
- Representational drawings of the crane
- Relevant fee

DESIGN VERIFIER — must NOT have been involved in designing the plant being verified.

CERTIFICATE OF REGISTRABLE PLANT DESIGN stops having effect if design is changed in a way that may affect health or safety of operators or nearby workers.

EXAMPLES of changes affecting certificate:
- Increasing number of counterweights to increase rated capacity = CERTIFICATE STOPS (may increase structural failure or overturn risk)
- Replacing hoist rope with different construction rope where manufacturer specifies suitability = CERTIFICATE CONTINUES (no safety impact)

MOBILE CRANES MUST be designed in accordance with acceptable engineering principles or relevant technical standards to ensure they are without risk to health and safety.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Mobile crane with rated capacity >10t on site without visible or accessible design registration documentation = flag
- Crane with altered design (e.g. additional counterweights) without documented re-registration = serious non-compliance
- Design verification statement signed by person involved in the design = non-compliance

CHUNK 6: Limiting and Indicating Devices — Technical Requirements
Section: CoP s4; AS 1418.5
Topic: Rated capacity limiters, motion limiting devices, load indicators, working radius indicators, free fall lock-out

LIMITING DEVICES — stop a specific crane motion before the crane moves into an unsafe situation.
INDICATING DEVICES — visually or audibly warn the crane operator of approaching limits or unsafe situations.

RATED CAPACITY LIMITERS:
- CRANES MANUFACTURED 2002–2013: required on all mobile cranes with MAXIMUM SWL MORE THAN 3 TONNES
- CRANES MANUFACTURED SINCE 2013: required on cranes with MAXIMUM RATED CAPACITY OF 1,000 KG OR 40,000 NM OR GREATER

Rated capacity limiter must prevent:
- Hoisting of a load GREATER THAN 110% of rated capacity
- Radius being increased GREATER THAN 110% at the particular radius

MOTION LIMITING DEVICES — must be fitted to prevent:
- Highest position of hook ("anti-two block")
- Extreme permissible operating positions of jib (luff limiter)
- End positions of horizontally telescoping or movable jibs

WORKING RADIUS INDICATOR:
- Should be fitted on all mobile cranes originally designed with this feature
- Displays radius of suspended load from centre of slew ring (or centre of front axle for pick-and-carry cranes)

LOAD INDICATORS:
- Must be fitted on all mobile cranes with MAXIMUM RATED CAPACITY OF 1,000 KG OR 40,000 NM OR GREATER
- Must be capable of displaying mass of suspended load AT ALL TIMES

ANEMOMETERS (wind speed measuring devices):
- Must be provided on all SLEWING MOBILE CRANES with MAXIMUM RATED CAPACITY OF 45 TONNES OR MORE
- Wind speed measuring device should be at TIP OF MAIN BOOM (unless crane manufacturer specifies otherwise)
- MANDATORY DEADLINE: Anemometers must be fitted on all cranes by 2 SEPTEMBER 2025

FREE FALL LOCK-OUT:
- Crane owners SHOULD DE-ACTIVATE free fall facility permanently unless no alternatives exist
- De-activation must include: removal of free fall switches AND a certificate from crane manufacturer or competent person (preferably professional engineer) confirming permanent removal
- Where switches cannot be removed: free fall function must be POSITIVELY LOCKED OUT with written certificate from competent person
- If free fall is used: documented risk assessment required; do not use same crane for general lifting without safety inspection between activities

CALIBRATION:
- Calibration of limiting and indicating devices should be checked at PERIODIC INTERVALS by qualified person per crane manufacturer's instructions

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Mobile crane in operation without load indicator = non-compliance (cranes ≥1,000 kg / 40,000 Nm)
- Rated capacity limiter absent on crane with SWL >3t (manufactured 2002–2013) = non-compliance
- No anti-two block device fitted = non-compliance
- Slewing crane ≥45t without anemometer (after 2 September 2025) = non-compliance
- Free fall feature operational on crane without documented risk assessment or lock-out certificate = flag
- Calibration certificates not current = flag

CHUNK 7: Ergonomic Issues — Safe Access, Seating, and Visibility
Section: CoP s5
Topic: Safe means of access to crane, operator seating requirements, cabin visibility

SAFE MEANS OF ACCESS:
- Mobile cranes should be fitted with safe means of access to the crane cabin and other frequently accessed areas
- Must include (as appropriate): ladders, footholds, steps, grab rails
- Access provisions provided by the crane manufacturer must NOT be removed or modified unless a qualified person (e.g. ergonomist) specifies otherwise
- Where risk of slips, trips and falls exists: may need to modify walking surfaces with anti-slip product (e.g. treads, paint)

SEATING:
- Design of seating should account for extensive periods of time in the seat
- Seating must be: comfortable, with adequate back support, and height adjustable

WINDOWS AND WINDSCREENS:
- Clear vision must be provided in operator's cabin at all times
- Access must be provided for regular cleaning
- CRACKED GLASS must be replaced (windows and windscreens)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Missing or damaged access ladders, steps or grab rails on crane = flag
- Operator cabin with cracked or obscured windscreen = non-compliance
- Anti-slip surfaces absent on access areas with slip risk = flag

CHUNK 8: Crane Documentation and Markings
Section: CoP s6; AS 1418.5
Topic: Load charts, operator's manual, crane markings requirements

LOAD CHARTS (Rated Capacity Charts):
- Must be provided with the crane
- Must be written in ENGLISH with values in METRIC (SI) UNITS
- Must include information specified in AS 1418.5: Cranes, hoists and winches – Mobile cranes
- Must include a note of what deductions apply (e.g. hook block, fly jib, lifting gear mass)
- Where crane has ONE MAIN LOAD CHART: must be fixed in operator's cabin in a clearly VISIBLE LOCATION
- Where crane has NUMEROUS LOAD CHARTS (different boom/fly jib configurations): hard copy or digital format of relevant configuration charts must be kept in operator's cabin
- LIFTING MUST NOT TAKE PLACE unless operator has access to a load chart (hard copy or electronic)

KEY LOAD CHART DEDUCTIONS TO CHECK:
1. Mass of hook block, lifting slings and other lifting gear must be SUBTRACTED from crane capacity at that radius (e.g. if crane can lift 20t and hook/gear = 1t, maximum load = 19t)
2. Mass of fly jib must be SUBTRACTED from main hook capacity when lifting from main hook with fly jib attached to boom head — unless the load chart specifically allows otherwise

LIFTING CAPACITIES specified on load chart MUST NEVER BE EXCEEDED — except during testing by a competent person under controlled conditions, or as documented and approved by the crane manufacturer.

CRANE OPERATOR'S MANUAL:
- Must be supplied with the mobile crane
- Must be written in ENGLISH
- Must be kept on the crane at ALL TIMES — hard copy or digital format

CRANE MARKINGS:
- Crane and lifting components must be marked PERMANENTLY AND LEGIBLY in accordance with AS 1418.5
- Markings must be in ENGLISH with values in SI UNITS
- All operator controls must be suitably marked to indicate function and operation — in English or international code

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No load chart in operator cabin = serious non-compliance; lifting must cease
- Load chart not in English or metric units = non-compliance
- Crane operator's manual absent from crane = non-compliance
- Operator controls without function markings = non-compliance
- No deduction made for hook block/lifting gear mass when reading load chart = serious non-compliance flag

CHUNK 9: Planning and Coordinating Mobile Crane Operations
Section: CoP s7; AS 2550.5
Topic: Pre-lift planning requirements, crane selection, crane crew, crane siting, communication, documented lifting procedures

PLANNING — must start as early as possible; involves consultation with all engaged in the work including: principal contractor/crane hirer, crane supplier, electricity entity, engineer, PCBU, crane operator.

KEY PLANNING ISSUES:
- Determining appropriate crane for the work
- Ensuring adequate workplace access
- Identifying appropriate crane location relative to buildings, structures and plant
- Liaising with electricity entities for overhead powerline control measures
- Confirming ground conditions are adequate (including location of underground services: power, water, gas, sewers, phone/data)
- Ensuring appropriate number of personnel for safe operations

CRANE SELECTION — assess against criteria:
- Weight, dimensions and lift radii of heaviest/largest loads
- Maximum lift height and radius, and weight of loads at those points
- Number and frequency of lifts
- Duration crane is required on site
- Type of lifting (e.g. precise placement)
- Type of carrier (depends on ground conditions and machine capacity in operating quadrants)
- Whether loads need to be walked or carried
- Gradient of supporting surface
- Whether loads will be suspended for lengthy periods
- Workplace conditions: ground, access roads/ramps, space for erection, obstacles

CRANE CREW — NUMBER determined by risk assessment addressing:
- Collision between cranes and other plant; coordination of multiple cranes
- Loads contacting structures (including scaffolding) or obstructing walkways
- Overhead powerlines
- Crane crew visibility of load; ability to safely load/unload materials and assess work areas

ADDITIONAL CONTROL MEASURES FOR POOR VISIBILITY situations:
- Redesign of lift or alternative crane
- Isolation of area during lift
- Anti-collision devices and systems
- Flagging and insulated covers on suspended powerlines
- Safety observer or 'spotter'
- Additional dogger (e.g. where single dogger could lose sight of suspended load)
- Cameras fitted to crane, hook or structure

IMPORTANT RULE: A crane operator must NOT undertake their own dogging work or supervise a trainee dogger — even if the operator holds a dogging licence.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No evidence of pre-lift planning consultation = flag
- Crane crew size not supported by risk assessment = flag
- Crane operator also acting as dogger = serious non-compliance
- Crane sited near overhead powerlines without planning consultation with electricity entity = flag

CHUNK 10: Crane Siting — Collision Hazards and Exclusion Requirements
Section: CoP s7.3; WHS Regulation
Topic: Hazards to consider when siting a mobile crane; collision prevention; proximity to aerodromes

COLLISION HAZARDS TO CONSIDER when siting:
- Overhead powerlines and other services
- Nearby structures
- Other cranes or high obstructions (including on adjacent workplaces, e.g. concrete placement booms)
- Other mobile equipment moving within the crane working area
- Proximity to AERODROMES AND AIRCRAFT FLIGHT PATHS for 'high' cranes

HAZARD STRIPING (ZEBRA STRIPING) on outriggers and stabilisers:
- Should be permanently marked on outrigger beams or hydraulic cylinders
- Striping angle: 30–60 DEGREES TO THE HORIZONTAL
- Striping width: 40–150 MM WIDE
- Two contrasting colours, one of which is RED, YELLOW OR WHITE
- NOTE: If inadequate room, dimensions may be decreased for vehicle-loading cranes and small mobile cranes

PROXIMITY TO AERODROMES:
- Local aerodrome operator must be contacted to ensure CASA requirements are met
- Where necessary, AIRCRAFT WARNING LIGHTS should be fitted to the HIGHEST PART of the crane
- Reference: www.casa.gov.au

CRANES ON PUBLIC ROADS:
- Traffic control procedures of the road controlling authority MUST be complied with

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Outriggers or stabilisers without hazard striping = flag (reduced visibility risk)
- Hazard striping colour does not include red, yellow or white = non-compliance
- Hazard striping angle outside 30–60 degrees or width less than 40mm = non-compliance
- No aircraft warning lights on crane operating in designated flight path area = non-compliance
- No traffic management plan for crane set up on public road = non-compliance

CHUNK 11: Communication During Crane Operations
Section: CoP s7.4; AS 2550.1
Topic: Communication requirements between crane operators and doggers; radio use; hand signals

RELIABLE COMMUNICATION METHOD between crane operator and dogger (and all parties in multiple crane lifts) is ESSENTIAL.

EFFECTIVE COMMUNICATION IS ESPECIALLY IMPORTANT when:
- Crane operator cannot see the load
- Crane operator cannot see the load's landing area
- Crane operator cannot see the path of travel
- Crane operator cannot see the dogger
- Crane operator cannot judge distance accurately
- Crane is near overhead powerlines

RADIO COMMUNICATION:
- ANALOG RADIOS recommended over digital radios (digital risk of delay causing cut-off/misinterpretation)
- Fixed channel radio frequency should be selected for the duration of crane operations
- Pre-lift safety check: confirm radios performing satisfactorily; fully charged battery AND spare available
- Operators must be familiar with workplace-specific radio procedures
- CONSTANT TALK METHOD: all involved aware of progress at all times
- Crane operator normally takes instructions from ONE PERSON ONLY (exceptions for specific arrangements)
- WORK MUST STOP IMMEDIATELY if there is a loss of radio communication

ALTERNATIVE COMMUNICATION (when radio cannot be used):
- Hand signals, verbal communication or whistle signals
- Must comply with AS 2550.1: Cranes, hoists and winches – Safe use – General requirements

MOBILE PHONES — MUST NOT be used for directing mobile crane operations (distraction risk: incoming calls, notifications).

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operator receiving directions via mobile phone = non-compliance
- No pre-lift radio check performed = flag
- Digital radios used without documented risk assessment for delay risk = flag
- No established communication protocol for multiple crane lift = non-compliance
- Work continuing after loss of radio communication = serious non-compliance

CHUNK 12: Documented Lifting Procedures — Requirements and Contents
Section: CoP s7.5; WHS Regulation s299
Topic: When documented lifting procedures are required; contents for comprehensive procedures

DOCUMENTED LIFTING PROCEDURES should be prepared for ALL mobile crane lifting operations.
- GENERIC procedures acceptable for straightforward, previously-undertaken lifts with similar circumstances
- COMPREHENSIVE SITE-SPECIFIC procedures required for complex and higher-risk lifts

SITUATIONS REQUIRING COMPREHENSIVE DOCUMENTED LIFTING PROCEDURES:
- Tilt-up or spin-up panel jobs
- Plant recovery (e.g. overturned plant)
- Multiple crane lifts (more than one crane lifting a load simultaneously)
- Lifting of workboxes with people
- Installation of bridge beams
- Working near live overhead powerlines
- Lifting large pressure vessels or tanks
- Using a crane for demolition
- Complex rigging arrangements (e.g. chain blocks attached to allow load rotation while suspended)
- Mobile cranes on barges
- Erection of tower cranes
- Heavy lifts: LOAD OF 50 TONNES OR MORE

CONTENTS OF COMPREHENSIVE LIFTING PROCEDURES — should include:
- Maximum load radius for each crane
- Spotter duties: what the duty is and who is responsible
- Position of load to be lifted and final position (diagram with plan view recommended)
- MAXIMUM WIND SPEED where load has large surface area
- Verification of MAXIMUM ALLOWABLE GROUND BEARING PRESSURE (for heavy lifts)
- Proximity hazards including underground services, retaining walls, embankments, crane clearance to structures and powerlines (best shown in plan or elevation drawings)
- Allowance for any factors requiring increased crane capacity (e.g. multiple crane lifts, demolition products)
- Rigging requirements
- May be a LIVE DOCUMENT allowing changes before and during the lift

NOTE: SWMS information may be incorporated into lifting procedures (or vice versa) to avoid duplication.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Complex or high-risk lift proceeding with only a generic lifting procedure = flag
- No lifting procedure at all for any crane operation = non-compliance
- Lifting procedure does not include ground bearing pressure verification for heavy lifts = flag
- No spotter duties documented for operations near powerlines = non-compliance

CHUNK 13: Crane Stability — Overview and Stabilising/Overturning Moments
Section: CoP s8, s8.1; AS 1418.5
Topic: Stability as a critical safety issue; stabilising vs overturning moments; load chart stability function; counterweights

STABILITY is one of the MOST IMPORTANT SAFETY ISSUES relating to mobile cranes. Failure to maintain stability is a KEY FACTOR in serious crane incidents.

MAIN STABILITY FACTORS:
- Stabilising moment — primarily provided by crane counterweight
- Overturning moment — applied by suspended load, boom outside tipping point, and wind
- Ground conditions and means of supporting outrigger pads or crane tyres
- Slope of ground (side slope and slope in direction of travel — especially for pick-and-carry cranes)
- Wind conditions — varies with size/shape of suspended load and crane boom

LOAD CHART STABILITY FACTORS (AS 1418.5):
- Stability factors in load charts allow for: dynamic factors from crane motion and load (e.g. boom movement, brake application, load swaying) and wind effects
- IMPORTANT: Some overseas standards (e.g. USA) specify LESS CONSERVATIVE stability factors than AS 1418.5 — if a crane is unknowingly set up to overseas standard, overturn risk is increased
- All mobile cranes must comply with AS 1418.5 stability factors; load charts must be marked as complying with this standard

COUNTERWEIGHTS:
- Counterweight too LIGHT: crane overturns toward suspended load
- Counterweight too HEAVY or wrong configuration may cause crane to fall BACKWARDS in situations such as:
  - Travelling up slope with boom luffed up
  - Inadequate timbers under outrigger pads below counterweight
  - Outriggers not extended or lowered into position

WHERE COUNTERWEIGHTS ARE REMOVABLE:
- Each counterweight must be clearly and permanently identified with: crane manufacturer's name or trademark AND mass of counterweight (preferably in tonnes)
- Correct type and number of counterweights must be attached for each lift
- Must be secured in the manner specified by the crane manufacturer

RATED CAPACITY LIMITER DATA INPUT:
- Data input must be correct for the COUNTERWEIGHT CONFIGURATION actually on the crane
- Data must relate to the appropriate load chart and boom configuration

ADDITIONAL COUNTERWEIGHTS beyond design:
- An engineer must check the complete crane design and CERTIFY it complies with AS 1418.5

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Counterweights not matching load chart configuration = serious non-compliance
- Removable counterweights without mass markings = non-compliance
- RCL computer not updated to reflect actual counterweight/boom configuration = flag
- No compliance marking on load chart referencing AS 1418.5 = flag

CHUNK 14: Ground Conditions — Ground Factors, Geotechnical Reports, and Proximity to Excavations
Section: CoP s8.2.1, s8.2.2, s8.2.3; WHS Regulation
Topic: Ground factors affecting crane stability; geotechnical reports; proximity rules for excavations and trenches

GROUND FACTORS THAT AFFECT STABILITY:
- Presence of water (mixed with soil as mud, or underground springs/streams)
- Type of ground (clay, sand, rock or mixture)
- BACKFILLED GROUND that was previously an excavation or trench
- Cavities or penetrations in ground covered but still existing
- Continued crane operation causing progressive sinking of outrigger pads

SURFACE CRUST WARNING:
- Ground with a firm surface crust may be SOFTER UNDERNEATH
- If outrigger punctures the crust: softer ground exposed = crane may overturn

GEOTECHNICAL REPORT:
- Where a geotechnical report is prepared for a building activity, it SHOULD BE PROVIDED to the crane supplier and crane operator
- Report should only be relied upon if it applies to the area where the crane will actually set up or travel
- For HEAVY LIFTS (bridge beams ≥10t and other lifts ≥50t): a SPECIFIC GEOTECHNICAL REPORT for the lifting activity must be prepared
- The PRINCIPAL CONTRACTOR or PCBU in control of site is responsible for engaging the geotechnical engineer
- NOTE: After rain or ground disturbance (e.g. trenching), the geotechnical report may NO LONGER BE RELEVANT

CRANE PROXIMITY TO EXCAVATIONS — general principles:

COMPACT AND NON-FRIABLE GROUND (not crumbling):
- Minimum horizontal distance from crane support timbers to excavation = DEPTH OF EXCAVATION (1H:1V rule)
- Example: 3m deep excavation = outrigger timbers must be AT LEAST 3M from trench edge

LOOSE OR BACKFILLED (CRUMBLING) GROUND:
- Minimum horizontal distance from crane support timbers to excavation = AT LEAST TWICE THE DEPTH of excavation (2H:1V rule)
- Example: 3m deep excavation in backfilled ground = outrigger timbers must be AT LEAST 6M from excavation wall

RISK FACTORS THAT INCREASE COLLAPSE RISK near excavations:
- Softer ground and groundwater presence
- Vertical cuts in excavation wall (more dangerous than battered walls)
- 'Slippery back' (naturally occurring slip plane/fracture in ground)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane outriggers set up on or near backfilled ground without geotechnical assessment = flag
- Crane proximity to open trench less than trench depth (non-friable ground) = serious non-compliance
- Crane proximity to backfilled excavation less than twice the depth = serious non-compliance
- No geotechnical report obtained for heavy lift (bridge beams ≥10t or loads ≥50t) = non-compliance
- Recent rain on site with no re-assessment of geotechnical conditions = flag

CHUNK 15: Outrigger Timbers, Pads and Bog Mats
Section: CoP s8.2.4; AS 1418.5
Topic: Materials for distributing crane load to ground; minimum dimensions; placement requirements

TYPES OF SUPPORT MATERIALS:
- TIMBERS (rectangular cross section) — most common under outrigger feet
- TIMBER AND PLASTIC PADS — provided for some cranes
- BOG MATS (usually steel plate) — used for heavier lifts; may be used under crawler crane tracks

MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS for timbers, pads, steel plates and bog mats:
- Minimum width: 200 MM
- Minimum thickness: 75 MM
- Timbers laid TOGETHER so width of timber pad is WIDER than the outrigger foot
- GAP BETWEEN TIMBERS: not to exceed 25 MM (bottom and top layers); 100 MM (intermediate layers)
- Risk of outrigger feet sliding off plastic pads or steel must be identified and controlled (e.g. on sloping road where level pad cannot be created)

TIMBERS MUST match specifications from:
- Crane manufacturer, OR
- A competent person (e.g. engineer) if manufacturer has not provided this information

CRAWLER CRANES vs OUTRIGGER CRANES:
- Crawler cranes generally apply LESS POINT LOAD than cranes on outriggers (large track contact area vs small outrigger contact area)
- However, for heavy lifts or poor ground bearing capacity, bog mats or other supporting materials may still be required for crawler cranes

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Timber width less than 200mm or thickness less than 75mm = non-compliance with minimum dimensions
- Timber pad narrower than outrigger foot = non-compliance
- Gaps between timbers exceeding 25mm (bottom/top) or 100mm (intermediate) = non-compliance
- Timber dimensions not specified by manufacturer or competent person = flag
- Crane outrigger foot on bare ground without any timber/pad = serious non-compliance

CHUNK 16: Performing Heavy Lifts — Ground Bearing Requirements
Section: CoP s8.2.5; AS 1418.5
Topic: Additional requirements for heavy lifts; geotechnical certification; calculating ground bearing pressure

HEAVY LIFTS requiring additional risk controls:
- BRIDGE BEAMS: 10 TONNES OR MORE
- TILT-UP PANELS (any weight)
- OTHER HEAVY LIFTS: 50 TONNES OR MORE

ADDITIONAL REQUIREMENTS for heavy lifts:
- CERTIFICATION of ground bearing capacity must be obtained from a GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEER before the lift
- Crane operator must COMPARE ground bearing capacity with maximum pressure the crane will apply to the ground
- GROUND BEARING CAPACITY MUST BE GREATER THAN MAXIMUM PRESSURE applied by crane
- Control measures such as BOG MATS may be used to distribute load and reduce ground pressure

MAXIMUM PERMISSIBLE GROUND PRESSURES (Table 1):
- Hard rock: 200 TONNES/M²
- Shale rock and sandstone: 80 TONNES/M²
- Compacted gravel (up to 20% sand): 40 TONNES/M²
- Asphalt: 20 TONNES/M²
- Compacted sand: 20 TONNES/M²
- Stiff clay (dry): 20 TONNES/M²
- Soft clay (dry): 10 TONNES/M²
- Loose sand: 10 TONNES/M²
- Wet clay: LESS THAN 10 TONNES/M²

GREATEST FORCE APPLIED by any outrigger occurs:
- At point of tipping (crane about to overturn)
- High boom angle with maximum counterweight and empty hook
- When crane boom is located directly above an outrigger pad

OUTRIGGER PRESSURE CALCULATION — Option 1 (force unknown):
Pout = 0.65 × (total crane mass + lifted load) ÷ individual outrigger area

Minimum area required:
area = 0.65 × (CM + L) ÷ PMAX

To find square timber dimensions: square root of area.

WORKED EXAMPLE 1:
- Crane mass: 40t, lifted load: 20t, timber area: 0.8m × 0.8m = 0.64m²
- Pout = 0.65 × 60 ÷ 0.64 = 60.9 tonnes/m²

WORKED EXAMPLE 2:
- Crane mass: 25t, lifted load: 10t, compacted gravel (PMAX = 40 t/m²)
- area = 0.65 × 35 ÷ 40 = 0.569m²; dimensions = √0.569 = 0.754m → 755mm × 755mm minimum

OUTRIGGER PRESSURE CALCULATION — Option 2 (outrigger force known):
- Pout = Force ÷ Area
- Minimum Area = Force ÷ PMAX

EXAMPLE 2 (Option 2): Crane with max outrigger load 25t on compacted gravel (PMAX = 40 t/m²)
- Area = 25 ÷ 40 = 0.625m²; dimensions = √0.625 = 0.79m → 790mm × 790mm minimum

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Heavy lift (bridge beams ≥10t, tilt-up panels, loads ≥50t) proceeding without geotechnical certification = serious non-compliance
- Ground pressure calculation absent for heavy lift = non-compliance
- Outrigger timbers too small for calculated ground pressure on existing ground type = serious non-compliance
- Crane set up on wet clay without significant bog mat support = high risk flag

CHUNK 17: Outriggers and Short-Legging
Section: CoP s8.2.6; AS 1418.5
Topic: Outrigger extension requirements; short-legging rules; partial extension

OUTRIGGERS vs STABILISERS:
- OUTRIGGERS: structural members that, when deployed, lift the crane's wheels off the ground
- STABILISERS: structural members that, when deployed, do NOT lift the crane's wheels off the ground

GENERAL RULES:
- Timbers or other load-distributing material MUST ALWAYS be placed under outriggers — irrespective of ground conditions
- Outriggers must be set according to manufacturer's operating instructions for the specific crane type
- Outriggers must be used to LEVEL the crane
- Where manufacturer specifies LOCKING PINS to secure extended outrigger: PINS MUST BE USED (especially important for two-stage outriggers)

CRANES NOT DESIGNED FOR LIFTING WITH PARTIALLY EXTENDED OUTRIGGERS:
- If one or more outriggers not fully extended: crane may become unstable
- SHORT-LEGGING ONLY PERMITTED where load charts are provided for partial outrigger extension
- Outriggers must be PERMANENTLY MARKED so that extension amount is clearly visible and corresponds to the relevant load chart
- Even if only one outrigger is partially extended: crane capacity for FULL 360 DEGREES of slew must not exceed the PARTIAL OUTRIGGER EXTENSION load chart capacity

CRANES WITH SLEW AND OUTRIGGER SENSING DEVICE:
- These cranes automatically limit lifting capacity based on outrigger configuration
- Different rated capacities can be applied through 360-degree rating, even with short-legged outriggers
- Information describing this operating feature must be KEPT ON THE CRANE AT ALL TIMES

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operating with one or more outriggers not fully extended without corresponding short-leg load chart = serious non-compliance
- Outrigger extension marks absent from outrigger beams = non-compliance
- Locking pins specified by manufacturer not fitted to outrigger extensions = non-compliance
- Outriggers deployed on bare ground without timbers or pads = non-compliance

CHUNK 18: Wind Effects on Mobile Crane Operations
Section: CoP s8.4; AS 1418.5; EN 13000
Topic: Maximum allowable wind speeds; wind direction effects; anemometers; engineering requirements for high-surface-area loads

GENERAL PRINCIPLE:
- Wind risk increases with: load radius, boom extension, boom height, and frontal area of load
- Loads that are LIGHT BUT HAVE A LARGE SURFACE AREA are most adversely affected by wind
- When wind speed DOUBLES: applied wind load INCREASES BY A FACTOR OF FOUR

MAXIMUM ALLOWABLE WIND SPEED:
- A maximum permissible wind speed of 10 M/SECOND (36 KM/HOUR) is specified by many manufacturers for some loads
- The maximum allowable wind speed stated by manufacturer is usually based on MAXIMUM WIND SPEED GUSTS not average wind speed
- Per EN 13000 standard: wind speed listed in capacity tables is the "3-SECOND WIND GUST" measured at the HIGHEST POINT OF THE BOOM
- The actual maximum allowable wind speed may be CONSIDERABLY LESS depending on load characteristics and lift configuration

WIND DIRECTION EFFECTS:
1. WIND FROM FRONT — pushes against boom; load indicator on LMS reads LIGHTER than actual load; safety cut-off functions may not engage correctly; load may damage crane
2. WIND FROM SIDE — does not change LMS reading but causes SIDE LOADING on boom/hoisting system; may push load outside stability limit
3. WIND FROM REAR — pushes against back of boom; load indicator reads GREATER than actual load; wind pushes load to INCREASE RADIUS, reducing crane capacity

ENGINEERING ASSESSMENT for reduced wind speed:
Required when load has large surface area or high drag coefficient. Engineer considers:
- Crane manufacturer's information for specific model
- Crane configuration for the lift
- Wind surface area of the load
- Shape and drag coefficient of load (flat surface perpendicular to wind = higher drag than cylindrical load)
- Maximum radius at which crane will operate
- Margin of crane capacity above actual load (greater margin = can withstand higher wind)

PROFESSIONAL ENGINEER REQUIREMENT:
- In Queensland, persons carrying out an engineering service must be registered as a professional engineer under the Professional Engineers Act 2002
- Crane operator is NOT EXPECTED to calculate reduced wind speed for large surface area loads — this must be done by an engineer

METEOROLOGICAL DATA:
- Bureau of Meteorology weather forecast should be assessed prior to critical lifts where wind is a key safety factor
- BoM data is normally at 10m above sea level — may not directly apply to crane lift height; allow safety margin

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Large surface area loads (e.g. fibreglass pool shells, panels, tanks) lifted without wind speed assessment = flag
- Crane operating after wind speed exceeds manufacturer's maximum or engineer's reduced limit = serious non-compliance
- No anemometer fitted to slewing crane ≥45t (after 2 September 2025) = non-compliance
- Load indicator reading not corresponding to actual load due to wind from front = dangerous condition; flag

CHUNK 19: Roles and Responsibilities — Crane Stability
Section: CoP s8.5
Topic: Specific stability responsibilities of crane operator, manufacturer/supplier, principal contractor/PCBU, crane owner, and dogger

CRANE OPERATOR — stability responsibilities:
- Operate within crane capabilities to ensure no overturn
- Operator MUST have final say on whether a lift should proceed
- Confirm: crane adequately supported, level within manufacturer's tolerance; materials under outrigger feet/tracks meet manufacturer's or owner's specifications; suspended load within rated capacity; functions operating properly including all motions, brakes, load moment systems and indicators; wind not excessive for load being lifted (especially large surface area loads at height)
- Regularly inspect ground to ensure continuous crane operation has not compressed ground unsafely
- Must not operate pick-and-carry crane on gradients exceeding those specified by manufacturer
- Operator is NOT expected to calculate reduced wind speed for large surface area loads — that is an engineering task

CRANE MANUFACTURER AND SUPPLIER:
- Must ensure crane complies with strength and stability requirements of the design standard
- Must carry out testing demonstrating stability based on AS 1418.5
- Must provide information on maximum load applied by outrigger feet to ground (range of loads for various configurations)
- For crawler cranes: must provide maximum load applied across tracks to ground
- Pick-and-carry cranes: must provide clear information on maximum SIDE SLOPE for safe operation and amount of de-rating for slopes up to 5 DEGREES
- Must provide documented information on maximum pressure applied by crane tyres or tracks to supporting surface

PRINCIPAL CONTRACTOR or PCBU:
- Must supply ALL INFORMATION to crane crew about: location of trenches, backfilled excavations, covered penetrations, and underground essential services
- Must make available documented ground bearing capacity information
- For heavy lifts (bridge beams ≥10t; tilt-up panels; loads ≥50t): MUST obtain documented geotechnical information from a geotechnical engineer
- Should NOT attempt to influence crane crew to perform a lift the crew considers unsafe

CRANE OWNER (PCBU):
- Must ensure timbers or pads supplied with crane will adequately support it
- Where ground bearing capacity is LESS THAN 10 TONNES/M²: crane owner must clearly state minimum ground bearing capacity required
- Must be given ground bearing capacity information from principal contractor/PCBU before supplying crane for heavy lifts

DOGGER — stability responsibilities:
- Communicate weight of load to crane operator (where known)
- Calculate working load limit of all ropes, slings, chains and accessories
- Take adequate precautions directing pick-and-carry crane across rough surfaces; check area for hazards
- Provide clear and accurate directions to crane operator

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Principal contractor unable to provide underground services information to crane crew = non-compliance
- Crane operator forced by principal contractor to proceed with unsafe lift = serious non-compliance
- Crane owner supplying crane for heavy lift without verifying ground bearing capacity = non-compliance
- Dogger not communicating load weight to crane operator = flag

CHUNK 20: Working Near Overhead Powerlines — Exclusion Zones
Section: CoP s9.1; ES Act s30; ES Regulation s68, s69
Topic: Planning requirements; exclusion zones; spotters; devices for powerline safety

LEGAL REQUIREMENT (ES Regulation s68):
- PCBU must ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, that no person, plant or thing at the workplace comes within an UNSAFE DISTANCE of an overhead electric line

HIERARCHY OF CONTROLS for overhead powerlines:
1. DE-ENERGISE or RE-ROUTE the powerline (most effective — must be considered first)
2. If not reasonably practicable: establish EXCLUSION ZONES

DE-ENERGISATION/RE-ROUTING:
- Arrange with relevant electricity entity as early as possible (takes time to arrange)
- PCBU, principal contractor, and crane owner must all be consulted about this option
- Written confirmation required from person in control of the powerline before undertaking any work where powerlines have been de-energised

EXCLUSION ZONES:
- Workers, crane and load must stay AT LEAST 3 METRES AWAY from overhead powerlines for voltages UP TO 132 KV
- GREATER DISTANCES apply for voltages above 132 kV (per ES Regulation Schedule 2)
- Electricity entities may specify GREATER distances than ES Regulation minimums
- Exclusion zone must NOT be breached regardless of safety devices fitted
- The identified minimum clearance distance may need to be GREATER than the prescribed zone (to account for load swing after crane stops)

FACTORS TO CONSIDER when implementing exclusion zone:
- Maximum travel distance of crane boom
- Size and shape of any load to be lifted
- Possibility of load swing after boom comes to rest
- Sway or sag of powerlines (sway from wind; sag varies with line temperature)

SAFETY OBSERVER ('SPOTTER') — required where crane operates near powerlines:
- Must observe operations and advise operator if crane or load may enter the exclusion zone
- Must NOT carry out other tasks (e.g. dogging duties) concurrently while acting as spotter
- Must have knowledge of: working safely around moving plant; escape routes; maintaining visibility; relevant traffic management guidelines; exclusion zones; how mobile cranes operate and their movement limits
- Training appropriate for spotters includes:
  - RIIRTM203E – Work as a safety observer/spotter
  - T0911 Introduction to Electrical Network Infrastructure for Authorised Persons
  - UETDREL006 – Work safely in the vicinity of live electrical apparatus as a non-electrical worker

DEVICES and ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS (lower order — only when higher controls not practicable):
- Warning signs indicating location of overhead powerlines
- Tiger tails or line markers (VISUAL AID ONLY — NOT insulation; only low voltage lines <1,000 V can be continuously covered)
- Warning devices alerting operator before boom enters exclusion zone
- Limiting devices must be designed to 'FAIL-SAFE'

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Any part of crane boom, jib or load within 3m of powerlines up to 132kV = serious non-compliance
- No spotter present when crane operates near overhead powerlines = non-compliance
- Spotter performing dogging duties while also acting as spotter = non-compliance
- Tiger tails fitted to high voltage lines with personnel assuming they are insulated = serious non-compliance
- Work proceeding near powerlines without written confirmation of de-energisation = serious non-compliance

CHUNK 21: Working Near Other Plant and Structures
Section: CoP s9.2; WHS Regulation
Topic: Collision prevention between cranes and other plant, structures or buildings

COLLISION RISKS between mobile crane and other plant/structures can result in:
- Dropped loads
- Overturning cranes
- Broken crane components (e.g. boom sections)

WHERE TWO OR MORE CRANES or other mobile plant work within a workplace or share airspace:
- A DOCUMENTED PROCEDURE (e.g. SWMS for construction work) MUST be established to ensure sufficient clearances
- Each work area should nominate a person responsible for implementing the collision control system

SYSTEMS OF WORK for adjacent cranes:
- Negotiated between persons in control from each work area
- Must ensure sufficient clearances between cranes, loads and mobile plant

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Multiple cranes sharing airspace without documented collision prevention procedure = serious non-compliance
- No nominated person responsible for collision prevention between cranes = flag
- Cranes operating near scaffolding, concrete placement booms or other structures without clearance confirmation = flag

CHUNK 22: Erecting and Dismantling Mobile Cranes
Section: CoP s10; WHS Regulation s54, s55
Topic: Responsibilities for erecting and dismantling; minimising collapse and falling object risks

LEGAL REQUIREMENT: Plant must NOT be commissioned or dismantled unless the person with management or control believes, so far as is reasonably practicable, it is without risks to health and safety.

PERSON WITH MANAGEMENT OR CONTROL must:
- Manage risks to health and safety during erecting/dismantling
- Ensure the person erecting or dismantling is a COMPETENT PERSON (e.g. a rigger)
- Provide all available information for eliminating or minimising risks

DOCUMENTED WORK PROCEDURE required that follows crane manufacturer's specifications for erecting and dismantling, ensuring:
- Crane erected/dismantled in accordance with designer's/manufacturer's instructions
- Access and egress comply with relevant technical standards
- Crane is stable during erecting and dismantling
- Method will not adversely affect other plant and structures
- Approved special tools, jigs and appliances are used
- Interaction of crane with other plant is considered
- Environmental factors (wet, windy conditions) are considered
- Workers are provided with suitable FALL PROTECTION when working at height

CRANE COLLAPSE PREVENTION:
- Only parts/components meeting manufacturer's or competent person's specifications may be used
- Components must be assembled in CORRECT SEQUENCE using appropriate tools per prescribed procedures
- When changing boom sections on LATTICE BOOM CRANES: adequate support must be provided under each section BEFORE REMOVING JOINT PINS or bolts
- When joint pins/bolts are replaced: must be provided with properly fitted SPLIT PINS or LOCKING DEVICE
- Counterweight must be attached BEFORE boom/jib is fitted or removed (per manufacturer's requirements)

FALLING OBJECTS (WHS Regulation s54, s55):
- Must prevent objects from falling freely so far as reasonably practicable, OR
- Where not reasonably practicable: provide a system to ARREST the fall
- Control measures:
  - Erect and maintain EFFECTIVE BARRICADES at appropriate distance around crane; only persons directly involved to be inside
  - Schedule erecting/dismantling when movement of other persons and plant is at MINIMUM

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane being erected or dismantled by person without rigger's or equivalent licence = non-compliance
- No fall protection for workers operating at height during erection/dismantling = non-compliance
- No barricades around crane during erection/dismantling = non-compliance
- Joint pins replaced without split pins or locking devices = non-compliance
- Boom sections changed without adequate support under sections = flag

CHUNK 23: Roles and Responsibilities — Operational Issues
Section: CoP s11.1; WHS Regulation
Topic: Specific operational duties of crane owner, principal contractor, crane operator and dogger

CRANE OWNER — operational duties:
- Ensure only persons with APPROPRIATE MOBILE CRANE LICENCE CLASS operate the crane
- Ensure operators, riggers and doggers have undergone FAMILIARISATION AND REFRESHER TRAINING
- Keep crane manufacturer's operating manual ON THE CRANE
- Maintain all other crane manufacturer's manuals and instructions
- Define roles and responsibilities for all persons involved with crane operation
- Ensure thorough equipment maintenance and inspection program is in place
- MUST NOT allow a person in charge of and/or operating a crane to also undertake their own dogging work or supervise a trainee dogger — even if the operator holds a dogging licence

PRINCIPAL CONTRACTOR — operational duties:
- Ensure overall coordination of lifting operations on construction site
- Ensure workplace is adequately prepared for incoming mobile crane (including ground conditions)
- Consult with crane operator on arrival at workplace
- Ensure all persons have site-specific induction before commencing work
- Sight general induction evidence for all PCBUs and workers before construction work starts
- Ensure crane operators and doggers hold appropriate HIGH RISK WORK LICENCES
- Be aware of location of powerlines and whether they have been de-energised before work commences
- Must NOT attempt to influence crane crew to perform a lift the crew considers unsafe
- For lifts using steel reinforcement cages: ensure there are ENGINEERED LIFTING POINTS

CRANE OPERATOR — operational duties:
- Primary role: safe operation of the crane at all times
- Must NOT proceed with a lift believed to be dangerous or unsafe until concern is reported and safe conditions confirmed
- Must know: specific model's characteristics, functions and limitations; load chart (including notes and warnings); how to calculate net capacity for the required configuration; adjustment of load limiting settings; inspection and maintenance procedures; workplace conditions affecting crane operation; whistle, hand and radio signals; basic slinging techniques
- Before and during operation: set up crane per manufacturer's instructions; complete daily inspection checklist and logbook; check no unauthorised persons on crane; inspect area including ground condition; monitor ground during repetitive lifts; have clear understanding of lift procedure; check each motion is safe
- MUST NOT (while operating): supervise/direct a trainee dogger; leave controls while load is suspended (except emergency); leave crane to sling own load
- These restrictions apply regardless of whether the operator also holds a dogging licence

DOGGER — operational duties:
- Must hold HIGH RISK WORK LICENCE for dogging work (WHS Regulation Schedule 3)
- Primary role: assist crane operator in safe and efficient crane operation — applying slinging techniques, selecting and inspecting lifting gear, directing crane operator when load is out of view
- Must be positioned to SAFELY OBSERVE the entire lifting operation they are responsible for
- A dogger MUST NOT concurrently perform the role of 'spotter' when crane is operating near overhead powerlines
- Must be in control of load from time it is slung until securely placed in final position
- If more than one dogger controls a load: each must know which part of the lift they are responsible for
- Before signalling operator to raise load: must confirm all attachments have appropriate WLL; lifting gear correctly applied; load secured and balanced; load free and not snagged; load will not contact any object when lifted
- Doggers must remain OUTSIDE THE CRUSH ZONE while load is being lifted

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operator acting as own dogger or supervising trainee dogger = serious non-compliance
- Dogger without valid high risk work dogging licence = non-compliance
- Dogger and spotter duties performed by same person near powerlines = non-compliance
- Crane operator leaving controls while load is suspended = non-compliance
- Dogger inside crush zone during lifting = serious safety risk

CHUNK 24: Integrity of Lifting Gear — Inspection and Use
Section: CoP s11.2.1; AS 2759, AS 3775.2, AS 2321, AS 4497, AS 1353.2, AS 4991, AS 2741
Topic: Types of lifting gear; inspection requirements; correct and incorrect slinging techniques

REFERENCED STANDARDS for lifting gear:
- AS 2759: Steel wire rope – Use, operation and guidance
- AS 3775.2: Chain slings – Grade T – Care and use
- AS 2321: Short-link chain for lifting purposes
- AS 4497: Round slings – Synthetic fibre
- AS 1353.2: Flat synthetic-webbing slings – Care and use
- AS 4991: Lifting devices
- AS 2741: Shackles

BASIC ITEMS TO CHECK on lifting gear:
- Lifting gear is TAGGED with all relevant information (e.g. chain sling: grade, WLL, manufacturer, chain size, AS marking)
- Lifting hooks have OPERABLE SAFETY LATCHES
- PERMANENTLY FIXED SHACKLES are prevented from unscrewing (e.g. mousing or similar)
- Lifting eyes and inserts are COMPATIBLE and SAME PROPRIETARY BRAND
- Slings are NOT DAMAGED (no excessive wear, damaged strands, cracks, deformation or severe corrosion)
- Sling is APPROPRIATE for the load (adequate capacity and protection from sharp edges)
- Load reduction factors applied where radius (R) of load corner is less than twice the link diameter (d) of lifting chain

CHAIN SLING LOAD DERATION FACTORS (Figure 13):
- R > 2d (larger than twice link diameter): Load Factor = 1 (full WLL applies)
- R > d but ≤ 2d: Load Factor = 0.7 (WLL = 70% of reeved WLL)
- R < d (smaller than link diameter): Load Factor = 0.5 (WLL = 50% of reeved WLL)

EXAMPLE CHAIN SLING DERATIONS:
- 13mm chain, reeved WLL = 4.0t: at R < d → WLL = 0.5 × 4.0 = 2.0t
- 19mm chain, reeved WLL = 8.4t: at R > d ≤ 2d → WLL = 0.7 × 8.4 = 5.88t
- 26mm chain, reeved WLL = 15.9t: at R < d → WLL = 0.5 × 15.9 = 7.95t

SLING PLACEMENT PRINCIPLES:
- Slings must NOT be crossed or twisted
- Lift point should be located DIRECTLY ABOVE CENTRE OF GRAVITY of load
- Protective sleeves and corner pieces per manufacturer's requirements

SYNTHETIC SLING USE:
- Round (sausage) slings should only be used for ROUND LOADS or other shapes with protective sleeves
- Sharp-edged loads may damage or tear round slings
- Rigid sling protection corner radius: at least 3 TIMES the COMPRESSED THICKNESS of the sling, or per sling manufacturer's recommendations (AS 4497)

INSPECTION FREQUENCY:
- All lifting gear (slings, hooks, material boxes): inspect periodically for damage and wear by a competent person — NOT EXCEEDING 12 MONTHS between inspections
- SYNTHETIC SLINGS: inspect at THREE-MONTHLY INTERVALS (AS 1353.2; AS 4497)
- ALL LIFTING GEAR must be tagged to identify date of last inspection (colour coding commonly used)
- Documented maintenance records for lifting gear must be available at workplace
- BEFORE EACH LIFT: dogger must inspect lifting gear for any damage or wear

INCORRECT SLINGING TECHNIQUES — NOT PERMITTED:
- Slings bearing against sharp edges of metal sheet or I-beam (may cut sling)
- Single wrap choke with greater than 90-degree inclusion angle on steel pipe (does not prevent slippage)
- Sling hook inserted into pipe
- Chain sling with chain bearing against latch

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Lifting gear without visible tags showing WLL and inspection date = non-compliance
- Lifting hooks without operable safety latches = non-compliance
- Fixed shackles not moused or otherwise prevented from unscrewing = non-compliance
- Slings bearing on sharp edges without protection = non-compliance
- Synthetic slings inspected less frequently than 3-monthly = non-compliance
- Chain sling used at corner with radius less than link diameter without applying deration factor = serious non-compliance

CHUNK 25: Crane Overload — Control Measures
Section: CoP s11.2.2; AS 1418.5
Topic: Preventing crane overload; pre-lift checks; operator procedures for maximum loads

FUNDAMENTAL RULE: A mobile crane must be operated WITHIN ITS RATED CAPACITY.

- Crane operator leaving cabin while load is suspended = non-compliance
- No load indicator check performed after lifting maximum or near-maximum load = flag

CHUNK 26: Unsecured and Dropped Loads — Control Measures
Section: CoP s11.2.3; WHS Regulation s55, s219, s315L, s315M
Topic: Exclusion zones; load securing requirements for various materials; reinforcement cages; bulker bags

LEGAL REQUIREMENTS:
- WHS Regulation s219: Person with management or control of plant must ensure, so far as reasonably practicable, that NO LOADS ARE SUSPENDED OR TRAVEL OVER A PERSON unless plant is specifically designed for that purpose
- WHS Regulation s315L: Principal contractor must ensure, before construction work starts, that adjoining areas where loads are lifted over are CLOSED, or a GANTRY is erected providing adequate protection

EXCLUSION ZONES:
- Must be established around mobile cranes and adjoining areas to prevent persons entering the area
- Size of exclusion zone must be based on a WRITTEN RISK ASSESSMENT
- Closure of public footpath or roadway: APPROVAL required from relevant authority; pedestrians must be directed to alternative footpath
- Lane closures and barricades must comply with Dept of Transport and Main Roads, local government and relevant building/local Act requirements

MATERIAL BOXES:
- Tare mass and SWL must be clearly marked on all material boxes (with distinct identification number cross-referenceable to design drawing or certificate)
- Must be ENGINEER-DESIGNED AND CERTIFIED
- FOUR CHAINS (one in each corner) must be attached to material boxes during lifting
- Must use specifically designed material boxes for smaller components with enclosed sides or robust mesh; openings less than minimum size of materials being lifted
- Must be inspected, maintained, with inspection records kept
- Loads within boxes must be SECURED AGAINST MOVEMENT
- Materials must NOT be stacked higher than the side of the box unless adequately secured; box must NEVER be top-heavy

GENERAL LIFTING — load securing:
- Formwork frames: tied together OR lifting slings wrapped around load
- Loads of joists or bearers: STRAPPED TOGETHER before lifting
- Timber sheeting: STRAPPED TOGETHER and lifted in a FLAT POSITION
- Plasterboard sheets: lift in specifically designed material box, OR engineer-certified system specifying minimum/maximum sheets, number and locations of slings, and sling capacity
- Tag lines must be used as required to control loads
- All loads should be SUPPORTED WITH DUNNAGE where possible
- BASKET HITCHES MUST NOT be used near persons unless sling is positively restrained from sliding along the load
- HIGH/LOW LOADS (multiple loads at the same time): AVOID — difficulty ensuring dogger is not underneath loads

REINFORCEMENT CAGES:
- Where risk of workers being struck by falling reinforcement cage: design of cage and lifting method must be CERTIFIED BY AN ENGINEER
- Engineer must consider: lifting loads over full range of motion and orientation; loads on lifting points, connections, tie wire, localised stress in bars; overall stability of cage or mat
- Lifting points, number of slings and method must be shown on a DRAWING supplied to site
- Lifting points must be CLEARLY MARKED on the cage

BULKER BAGS (flexible intermediate bulk containers):
- Per AS3668: PERSONNEL MUST NOT be underneath bulker bags
- Slings on bags should only be used for lifting when EXCLUSION ZONE is in use with no possibility of any person being underneath
- For lifts other than ground-level transfers where exclusion zone is not in place: bulker bags must be lifted in BRICK CAGES or MATERIAL BOXES

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Persons under suspended loads = serious non-compliance
- No exclusion zone around crane during lifting operations = non-compliance
- Material boxes without SWL and tare mass markings = non-compliance
- Material boxes with fewer than four chains during lifting = non-compliance
- Basket hitches used near personnel without positive restraint = non-compliance
- Bulker bags lifted without exclusion zone or protective cage/box = serious non-compliance
- Steel reinforcement cages lifted without engineering certification = serious non-compliance
- Loads stacked higher than sides of material box without securing = non-compliance

CHUNK 27: Multiple Crane Lifts — Planning and Safety Requirements
Section: CoP s11.3; AS 1418.5
Topic: When multiple crane lifts occur; safety measures; calculated share of load; minimum capacity margins

MULTIPLE CRANE LIFT — any lift where two or more cranes lift a load simultaneously.
- Higher risk than single crane lift due to relative motion between cranes creating additional loadings
- A LIFT PLAN (documented lifting procedure and SWMS where applicable) must be developed

SAFETY MEASURES before multiple crane lift:
- DOCUMENTED LIFTING PROCEDURE prepared, preferably with DIAGRAMS showing crane and load positions
- Person DIRECTLY SUPERVISING the lift must hold HRWL in INTERMEDIATE OR ADVANCED RIGGING
- Accurate assessment of: share of load for each crane; how load share will be maintained
- Instructions to each crane operator must be CLEAR; responsibilities understood by each person
- Select cranes of SIMILAR CAPACITY AND CHARACTERISTICS where practicable
- Ensure all cranes have CORRECTLY CALIBRATED LOAD INDICATORS

MINIMUM CRANE CAPACITY REQUIREMENTS (calculated share of load):
- TWO cranes: each crane capacity must be AT LEAST 20% GREATER than calculated share
- THREE cranes: each crane capacity must be AT LEAST 33% GREATER than calculated share
- FOUR OR MORE cranes: each crane capacity must be AT LEAST 50% GREATER than calculated share
- If not practicable to comply: an ENGINEER must CHECK AND CERTIFY the lifting procedure; must be documented

LOAD SPIKING — a risk when lowering:
- If one end of load contacts ground before the other end: momentary load on the crane suspending the opposite end can be 33% HIGHER than calculated share
- Both ends/corners of load must be lowered SIMULTANEOUSLY

DUAL LIFT (pick-and-carry) — specific safety principles:
- Position cranes SIDE BY SIDE or IN LINE where no obstructions restrict paths
- Choose travel path where SHARP TURNS ARE AVOIDED
- Keep load as CLOSE TO GROUND AS POSSIBLE while remaining clear of ground
- Drive both cranes at SAME SLOW TRAVEL SPEED; avoid rapid brake application
- When one crane behind the other: LEAD CRANE controls pace; following crane adjusts speed to keep hoist ropes vertical
- Avoid two-blocking hooks; allow adequate hoist rope length so operator can see if rope is not hanging close to vertical
- When moving around a corner (one crane in front, one behind): front crane must NOT PULL second crane over (greater hazard with articulated crane)

FACTORS TO CONSIDER in multiple crane lift planning:
- Mass of the load and position of centre of gravity
- Mass of lifting gear
- Working load limit of lifting gear
- Synchronisation of crane motions (always some variation in practice — perform at LOW SPEEDS)
- Communication method (two-way radios required if crane operator not visible during lift)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Multiple crane lift without documented lifting procedure and diagrams = serious non-compliance
- Person supervising multiple crane lift without intermediate or advanced rigging HRWL = non-compliance
- Crane capacity for multiple lift not meeting the minimum percentage margin above calculated share = serious non-compliance
- No engineer certification where minimum capacity margins cannot be achieved = non-compliance
- Multiple crane lift performed at normal/fast speed without synchronisation controls = flag

CHUNK 28: Leaving Crane Unattended and Road Travel
Section: CoP s11.4, s11.5; Transport Operations (Road Use Management) Act 1995
Topic: Securing unattended cranes; road travel preparation; licensing requirements for driving mobile cranes

SECURING UNATTENDED CRANE — before leaving crane unattended:
- All loads removed from hook
- Hook raised to position safely clear of other operations
- All powered motions DISABLED
- KEYS REMOVED from crane
- CABIN LOCKED
- For longer periods: configure crane in OUT-OF-SERVICE condition per manufacturer's instructions

ROAD TRAVEL PREPARATION:
- Outriggers (hydraulic and manual): secured with locking device specified by manufacturer; stowed in TRAVELLING POSITION (no lateral movement)
- Loose components: stored in appropriate storage areas per manufacturer's instructions
- All drives to hydraulic pumps, booms and outriggers: DISENGAGED; controls in OFF position
- Boom: restrained per manufacturer's instructions (no unintended movement)
- Hook block: restrained per manufacturer's instructions
- For cranes requiring dollies: slew brake disengaged; dolly securely fastened to crane connection points; luff cylinder energised/de-energised per manufacturer's instructions
- Fluid levels and tyre pressures checked
- For hydraulic suspension: maximum suspension system pressures per manufacturer must be understood

ROAD TRAVEL — DRIVER COMPETENCIES required:
- Start, steer, manoeuvre, position and stop in accordance with traffic regulations and vehicle handling procedures
- Manage braking system efficiently for greater stopping distances (size, weight and forward boom projection)
- Identify and control driving hazards (defensive driving)
- Park, shut down and secure per traffic regulations
- Maintain crash avoidance space
- Drive to conditions (rain, winding roads, unsealed)

ARTICULATED STEERING CRANES — additional requirements:
- Must be able to TEST emergency steering system
- Aware of need for slower speeds on winding or poor condition roads
- Know how to avoid losing control and what action to take if steering malfunctions
- Allow for INCREASED BOOM OVERHANG at front when stopping at intersections

DRIVER LICENSING REQUIREMENTS (Transport Operations (Road Use Management) Act 1995):
- Mobile crane ≤8t GVM (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM): CLASS LR
- Mobile crane >8t GVM with maximum 2 axles (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM): CLASS MR
- Mobile crane >8t GVM with more than 2 axles (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM): CLASS HR
- Mobile crane >8t GVM with more than 2 axles with a trailer (no GVM restriction): CLASS HC
- All mobile cranes with or without trailers: CLASS MC

NOTE: HRWL to operate a mobile crane DOES NOT authorise driving on a road — appropriate heavy vehicle CLASS DRIVER LICENCE is also required.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane left unattended with load on hook or cabin unlocked = non-compliance
- Crane on road with outriggers not stowed in travelling/locked position = serious non-compliance
- Mobile crane driven on road by person without appropriate GVM-based driver licence class = non-compliance
- Boom not restrained for road travel = flag

CHUNK 29: Workboxes and First Aid Boxes
Section: CoP s11.6; AS 1418.17; AS 2550.1
Topic: Features and use of crane-lifted workboxes and first aid boxes; crane requirements for workbox use

WORKBOXES — crane-lifted personnel carrying devices:
- MUST be used only after risk assessment demonstrating other means (scaffolding, elevating work platforms) are impractical
- GENERALLY do not provide same level of safety as properly erected scaffolding or EWPs

WORKBOX REQUIREMENTS:
- Must be DESIGN REGISTERED
- Correctly tagged lifting slings supplied and attached to lifting points via HAMMERLOCKS or MOUSED SHACKLES
- Factor of safety for each suspension sling: AT LEAST 8 FOR CHAINS; AT LEAST 10 FOR WIRE ROPE
- Must have FALL-ARREST ANCHORAGE POINTS
- SWL, tare mass and design registration number clearly marked (e.g. on data plate)
- Doors (if provided): INWARD OPENING ONLY; self-closing; latched to prevent accidental opening
- Sides: AT LEAST 1 METRE HIGH
- Handrail provided running around inside of box perimeter
- If fall risk exists: FALL-ARREST HARNESS must be provided and worn; harnesses attached to fall-arrest anchorage points in box or to main sling ring above workers' heads; energy absorbers required on lanyards (AS/NZS 1891 Series)
- At least ONE person in workbox must hold DOGGER or RIGGER LICENCE CLASS

FIRST AID BOX REQUIREMENTS (crane-lifted, for retrieval of injured persons):
- Must be DESIGN REGISTERED
- Clearly identified and marked as first aid boxes
- Provided with sides and a roof (horizontal gap permitted around all sides)
- Sides: AT LEAST 1 METRE HIGH to a horizontal gap; horizontal gap MUST NOT EXCEED 250 MM vertical distance
- Minimum internal HEIGHT: 2.2 METRES
- Minimum internal WIDTH: 1.2 METRES (face to face, not including grab rail)
- Minimum internal LENGTH: 2.8 METRES
- Maximum mesh size: 50 MM × 50 MM (openings not to exceed 0.0025 m²)
- Grab rail along longer sides, fitted internally
- Doors (if provided): may be outward opening but MUST BE LOCKABLE
- Persons in first aid box NOT required to use fall-arrest harnesses (box is enclosed)
- Slings attached via hammerlocks or moused shackles; factor of safety: AT LEAST 8 (chains) or AT LEAST 10 (wire rope)
- SWL, tare mass and design registration number must be marked
- At least ONE person in first aid box must hold DOGGER or RIGGER LICENCE CLASS
- Do NOT use when wind speeds exceed manufacturer's specification or in adverse weather (electrical storms)

CRANE REQUIREMENTS when using workboxes or first aid boxes:
- Rated capacity AT LEAST TWICE the total load of workbox and contents; minimum NOT LESS THAN 1,000 KG at maximum radius
- Must be fitted with UPPER HOIST LIMIT stopping operation of hoist and luff functions
- Levers and foot pedals fitted with CONSTANT PRESSURE SYSTEM (crane stops when operator removes pressure from controls)
- Crane must NOT be capable of FREE FALL

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workbox without design registration = non-compliance; use must cease
- Workbox side height less than 1 metre = non-compliance
- Door opening outward = non-compliance
- Workers in workbox without fall-arrest harnesses where fall risk exists = non-compliance
- Crane capacity less than twice total workbox load = serious non-compliance
- Crane with free fall capability used with workbox = serious non-compliance
- First aid box internal dimensions below minimums (height < 2.2m, width < 1.2m, length < 2.8m) = non-compliance
- First aid box used in high wind or electrical storm = flag

CHUNK 30: Fatigue Management
Section: CoP s11.7; WHS Act s19
Topic: Managing fatigue risks for crane crews; control measures; signs of fatigue

PCBU OBLIGATION: Must ensure workers are not exposed to health and safety risks, including RISK OF FATIGUE.

FACTORS INCREASING FATIGUE RISK:
- Early shift starts
- Late finishes
- Short breaks between shifts
- Long hours
- Work requiring continued physical effort
- Environmental conditions (hot climates)

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS of fatigue:
- Excessive yawning or falling asleep at work
- Short term memory problems; inability to concentrate
- Reduced capacity for effective interpersonal communication
- Impaired decision-making and judgment
- Reduced hand-eye coordination or slow reflexes
- Behaviour changes (e.g. repeatedly arriving late)
- Increased rates of unplanned absence

CONTROL MEASURES for fatigue:
- Working-hours policy including: maximum daily hours; maximum average weekly hours; maximum hours over selected period; policies for on-call work and work-related travel
- Procedures to manage and limit excessive hours (minimum breaks, especially during long shifts)
- Avoid work arrangements providing incentives for excessive hours
- Consider whether night work is necessary; reschedule non-essential work away from night
- Encourage workers to report fatigue concerns
- Contingency plans for situations where workers unexpectedly work longer hours
- Avoid work during extreme temperatures; use job rotation to minimise exposure
- Fatigue policy for ALL workers including managers and supervisors

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operator visibly fatigued (yawning, slow responses, inattention) = immediate risk; work must stop
- No documented working-hours policy on site = flag
- Workers without adequate break between shifts = flag
- Night crane operations without fatigue risk assessment or controls = flag

CHUNK 31: Mobile Cranes in Specific Applications — Demolition, Tree Lopping, Bridge Beams, Barges
Section: CoP s12; WHS Regulation s299
Topic: Special requirements for cranes used in demolition, tree lopping, bridge beam installation, and on barges

MOBILE CRANES FOR DEMOLITION:
- SWMS must be prepared before work commences
- Demolition with WRECKING BALL attached to mobile crane: MUST NOT be undertaken (high risk of damage)
- Additional safety factors in demolition:
  - Unknown exact mass of parts to be removed
  - Additional shock loading when structure disconnected (unbolted or cut with oxy torch)
  - Load may not be balanced (unusual shape)
  - Load may become stuck despite apparent removal of all restraint
- Due to these factors: mobile cranes for demolition must have RATED CAPACITY NOT LESS THAN 1.5 TIMES THE ASSESSED LOAD
  - EXAMPLE: 4t assessed load → rated capacity required = 6t MINIMUM (plus hook block and rigging)
- Traffic management must be implemented to prevent collision with pedestrians or other plant

MOBILE CRANES FOR TREE LOPPING:
- RECOMMENDED THAT MOBILE CRANES NOT BE USED for tree lopping
- If used, ALL of the following must be ensured:
  - Crane operator does not perform role of dogger
  - A dogger is present during all lifting activities
  - Arborist, crane operator and dogger consult with each other
  - Side loading will NOT be applied to crane boom
  - Any loading to the crane is LESS THAN 50% OF THE CRANE'S RATED CAPACITY
  - Crane will NOT be shock-loaded
  - Wind will not adversely affect safe crane use
  - Only vertical loads applied to hoist rope and crane hook
  - At completion of saw cut, radius of load will NOT increase
- If conditions can be ensured: work procedure must be documented with hazard control measures listed
- Arborist and crane operator must participate in and be satisfied with the documented procedure

VESSEL-MOUNTED (BARGE) CRANES:
- Vessel must be ANCHORED during crane operation (or anchored to craft alongside if not possible)
- Maximum list under rated capacity: MUST NOT EXCEED ±5 DEGREES with one half of freeboard remaining
- Manufacturer's rated capacities on vessels are frequently NOT GREATER THAN 70% OF LAND-BASED RATED CAPACITY
- Crane and vessel combination must be CERTIFIED BY A MARINE SURVEYOR per AMSA requirements
- Where crane can move along deck: lifting must only take place when crane is SECURED TO THE DECK (e.g. by chains of adequate strength)

MOBILE CRANES ON SUSPENDED STRUCTURES (floors/slabs):
- Prior to accessing: WRITTEN DOCUMENTATION must demonstrate design floor loading is not exceeded for point and distributed loads
- Where floor requires strengthening (e.g. propping): an ENGINEER must provide written instructions specifying dimensions, locations and other specifications of propping
- If crane can only be used in particular areas: access to other areas must be PREVENTED by barricades or barriers

LIFTING AND LANDING PRE-CAST BRIDGE BEAMS:
- Comprehensive lifting and installation procedures required
- Dual crane lift: INTERMEDIATE RIGGER (minimum) required to oversee
- Asymmetrical beams: position of lifting inserts and slings must be calculated so beams hang for easy installation
- Asymmetrical beams can become unstable when load is taken off crane hook: PCBU must supply method of securing beam to prevent rotation or falling
- Temporary securing method must be DOCUMENTED AND APPROVED BY AN ENGINEER experienced in rigging practices for bridge construction

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane used for demolition with rated capacity less than 1.5× assessed load = serious non-compliance
- Crane used for tree lopping with load exceeding 50% of rated capacity = non-compliance
- Crane on barge without marine surveyor certification = non-compliance
- Crane on barge with vessel list exceeding 5 degrees = serious non-compliance
- Crane operating on suspended floor without written engineering documentation = serious non-compliance
- Pre-cast bridge beams lifted by dual crane without intermediate rigger supervision = non-compliance
- Asymmetrical bridge beams without temporary securing method certified by engineer = non-compliance

CHUNK 32: Use of Earthmoving Plant as a Mobile Crane
Section: CoP s13; WHS Regulation s219; AS 1418.8
Topic: When earthmoving plant may be used as a crane; rated capacity; load charts; lifting points; quick hitches; burst protection

LEGAL REQUIREMENT (WHS Regulation s219):
- Person with management or control of plant must ensure plant used is SPECIFICALLY DESIGNED TO LIFT or suspend the load
- If not reasonably practicable: must ensure alternative plant does NOT cause GREATER RISK than specifically designed plant

EARTHMOVING PLANT USED AS CRANE includes: backhoes, front-end loaders, excavators, telescopic handlers.

WHEN EARTHMOVING PLANT IS NOT APPROPRIATE for lifting:
- When lifted objects become larger and taller and are not inherently stable when placed on level ground
- When workers are required to be in close proximity during the lift
- BACKHOES: NOT SUITABLE to move a suspended load in pick-and-carry mode (excessive load swing, exacerbated by wheels on rough ground)
- Earthmoving plant must NOT be used to lift personnel unless specifically designed to do so

STABILITY CRITERIA when used in crane mode (AS 1418.8):
- Maximum rated capacity must NOT exceed:
  - 75% OF TIPPING LOAD in STATIONARY mode
  - 66% OF TIPPING LOAD in PICK-AND-CARRY mode
  - 50% OF TIPPING LOAD for ARTICULATED WHEEL LOADERS and TOOL CARRIERS

LOAD CHART REQUIREMENTS:
- Load chart must be FIXED INSIDE OPERATOR'S CAB
- Must show: manufacturer's name and model; boom and dipper arm identification and length; track width (if variable); deductions for attachments (e.g. quick-hitch, bucket) so net allowable load can be determined; rated load at least stable position or means to determine load position; reduction in capacity for out-of-level operation or pick-and-carry mode

LIFTING POINTS:
- Supplied by plant manufacturer or, if not, designed by A SUITABLY QUALIFIED ENGINEER
- Must form a CLOSED EYE to which a load-rated shackle may be attached
- HOOKS MUST NOT be used on dipper arm or attachments (load may disengage as arm rotates — even with latch, which may be damaged)
- Welded lifting lugs: material specifications must be compatible for welding; appropriate welding procedures must be followed
- LIFTING LUGS MUST NOT be attached to BUCKETS
- Static strength test at 200% OF RATED CAPACITY must be carried out at each lift point; no permanent deformation permitted after testing

QUICK HITCHES:
- Must comply with AS 4772 or ISO 13031 (or be fitted with manual locking pin)
- Must be compatible with the mobile plant (pin size, rating etc.)
- Where quick hitch has a lifting lug: lifting point must be in form of CLOSED EYE with identification plate stating maximum rated lifting capacity
- Lifting point: a SWIVEL must be provided between the lifting hook and shackle on lifting lug
- Manual locking pin: head must be painted RED with lynch pin at opposite end
- Inspection: in accordance with manufacturer's instructions by competent person; includes tolerance checking of all critical components
- Where supplier claims compliance with AS 4772 or ISO 13031: CERTIFICATION DOCUMENT must be provided on request

BURST PROTECTION:
- Must be fitted on all earthmoving plant used as crane WHERE RATED CAPACITY EXCEEDS 1 TONNE
- Must be fitted to BOTH BOOM AND DIPPER ARM
- Must comply with ISO 8643 performance requirements for excavators and backhoe loaders
- Single rated capacity: rated capacity marked on boom or dipper arm; must be strictly observed at all times
- Variable rated capacities: manufacturer's load chart must be fixed in operator's cab; capacity at MINIMUM RADIUS used to decide if burst protection is required
- Where possible: burst protection device should NOT have an operator switch (risk of forgetting to switch on)
- Where rated capacity ≤1 TONNE and decision not to fit burst protection: plant must NOT be used to lift loads presenting risk to workers

EARTHMOVING PLANT SAFETY INSPECTIONS:
- Must be serviced and inspected per manufacturer's instructions
- Safety inspection at intervals NOT EXCEEDING 12 MONTHS regardless of working environment
- When used in crane mode: PRE-START INSPECTION must take place
- Inspections include: manufacturer-specified items; hydraulic fluid leaks; function testing; inspection of welds, pins, linkages on boom, dipper arm and hydraulic cylinder mounts for cracking, deformation and excessive play; pin retainers and locking systems; where applicable, quick-hitch function and tolerance checking
- Cracking found: replacement or repair certified by professional engineer or plant manufacturer

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Excavator or backhoe with load suspended without burst protection (>1t capacity) = non-compliance
- Lifting lug or hook attached to bucket = non-compliance
- Hook used on dipper arm instead of closed eye lifting point = non-compliance
- Quick hitch without manual locking pin and not compliant with AS 4772/ISO 13031 = non-compliance
- Rated capacity of earthmoving plant in crane mode exceeding 75% of tipping load (stationary) or 66% (pick-and-carry) = non-compliance
- No load chart in operator's cab for earthmoving plant used as crane = non-compliance
- Backhoe moving a suspended load in pick-and-carry mode = non-compliance

CHUNK 33: Vehicle-Loading Cranes — Requirements
Section: CoP s14; AS 1418.11
Topic: VLC general use; crane and vehicle suitability; controls; rated capacity limiters; stabiliser locking; operational requirements

VEHICLE-LOADING CRANE (VLC) — crane mounted on a vehicle for loading/unloading; also used for traditional crane operations.

CRANE AND VEHICLE SUITABILITY:
- VLCs should only be mounted on vehicle types and models SPECIFIED BY THE CRANE MANUFACTURER
- Welding crane to vehicle chassis is GENERALLY UNACCEPTABLE (can damage chassis; risk of fatigue failure)
- Mounting method must be in accordance with crane manufacturer's specifications or recommendations of a competent person
- Second-hand imported VLCs introduced to Queensland: crane and vehicle combination must have COMPLIANCE PLATE attached by authorised person per Dept of Transport and Main Roads requirements

CONTROLS:
- Must be CONSTANT PRESSURE (DEADMAN) type
- Permanently marked with clearly visible symbols per AS 1418.11
- Position and layout designed to MINIMISE CRUSH RISK from inadvertent operation
- EMERGENCY STOPPING DEVICE at EVERY CONTROL STATION:
  - Removes energy supply and brings crane to complete stop when activated
  - Readily visible and coloured RED
  - Located for easy access without exposing operator to other hazards
  - Locks in 'stop' position when activated

RATED CAPACITY LIMITERS (VLCs):
- Required on all VLCs with MAXIMUM RATED CAPACITY OF 1 TONNE OR GREATER, OR GROSS LIFTING MOMENT OF 40 kNm OR GREATER
- Purpose: prevent movements increasing load moment in excess of rated capacity; prevent increase of load radius or permissible structural stresses
- Where smaller VLCs not fitted with RCL: RELIEF VALVES and fittings used for overload protection

RATED CAPACITY INDICATORS:
- All VLCs manufactured after 2003 must be fitted with a RATED CAPACITY INDICATOR
- Must warn crane operator when load exceeds 90% OF RATED CAPACITY
- Must give SEPARATE WARNING to operator and persons nearby if rated capacity is being exceeded
- Both approach and exceed warnings must be CONTINUOUS and CLEARLY DISTINGUISHABLE from each other

STABILISER LOCKING:
- Stabilisers on VLCs are typically manually operated
- Locking mechanism for manually extended stabilisers must include TWO SEPARATE LATCHING DEVICES per stabiliser; AT LEAST ONE must be AUTOMATICALLY OPERATED (e.g. manually operated securing pin + automatic spring latch)
- If VLC does not have a WARNING SYSTEM for unlocked stabilisers in transport position: owners should RETROFIT a warning system
- AS 1418.11 requires: indicator visible and audible to driver in truck cabin (warning light + buzzer)
- Feasibility of upgrading warning/indicating system on older VLCs should be addressed during annual inspection
- Workers driving/operating vehicles must receive appropriate instruction, training and supervision for the specific make and model — DOCUMENTED

OPERATIONAL ISSUES for VLCs:
- Operators of VLCs with maximum load moment capacity of 10 METRE TONNES OR MORE must hold HRWL class CV to operate
- Person operating VLC under 10 metre tonnes MAY sling loads without dogging HRWL only when: no judgment required for slinging or inspection/selection of lifting gear; weight of load, slings and slinging technique are predetermined by a licensed dogger, rigger or qualified engineer; PCBU provides comprehensive documented training
- If load is out of operator's view: load movement must be directed by a QUALIFIED DOGGER OR RIGGER
- For PRECISION PLACEMENT of load: a dogger must be used
- Crane may ONLY be used with ALL STABILISERS EXTENDED per manufacturer's instructions
- Crane should only be used LEVEL per manufacturer's specifications (usually NOT EXCEEDING 1 DEGREE OR LESS)
- TIMBERS or pads specified by manufacturer must be provided under stabiliser feet
- Hooks must have SPRING-LOADED SAFETY LATCHES; adequately maintained
- WHERE SIDE LOAD POSSIBLE on crane hook: hook must have an APPROPRIATE SWIVEL
- Crane must NEVER be used in PICK-AND-CARRY MODE
- Where VLCs used to lift persons: must comply with AS 1418.10
- Spring lock-outs on vehicle must be activated during crane operation (where provided)
- Crane only to be used with load suspended VERTICALLY from hook; must NOT drag loads across supporting surface
- Stabiliser legs must be clearly marked with ZEBRA STRIPING (see Section 7.3.1 for dimensions)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- VLC stabilisers not fully extended during crane operation = non-compliance
- VLC tilted more than 1 degree during lifting = non-compliance
- VLC used in pick-and-carry mode = serious non-compliance
- No emergency stop device (coloured red) at each control station = non-compliance
- Rated capacity indicator absent on VLC manufactured after 2003 = non-compliance
- No two separate latching devices on VLC stabilisers = non-compliance
- No in-cab warning system for unlocked stabilisers = flag
- Load out of operator's view with no dogger or rigger directing = non-compliance
- VLC with load moment ≥10mt operated without CV class HRWL = non-compliance
- Stabiliser legs without zebra striping = flag

CHUNK 34: Training — Familiarisation and Refresher Training
Section: CoP s15; WHS Act s19(3)(f); WHS Regulation s39, s317
Topic: Responsibilities for training; familiarisation training requirements; refresher training frequency and records

INFORMATION, TRAINING AND INSTRUCTION must cover at least:
- Documented work procedures for setting up and safe operation of mobile crane activities
- Method for inspection and maintenance of mobile cranes
- Knowledge of crane manufacturer's operation and service manuals
- Correct use, care and storage of PPE
- Correct use, care and storage of tools and equipment
- Electrical safety practices
- Procedures in event of incident or injury
- Driving techniques for articulated mobile crane

FAMILIARISATION TRAINING:
- Required BEFORE a person is allowed to work as a crane operator at a new PCBU, or operating a newly acquired crane
- PCBU should either: assess the person's knowledge/understanding; seek evidence of competence; or provide additional training
- Representative from mobile crane supplier or manufacturer should be present when crane is new; must have detailed knowledge of operational and safety features; must be endorsed by supplier or manufacturer as competent to provide training
- A RECORD must be made and kept by PCBU AND crane operator
- Record signed by both the crane operator AND the PCBU or representative
- Record must be in the FORMAT OF A CHECKLIST

REFRESHER TRAINING:
- Required for ALL crane crew: crane operators, doggers and riggers
- Purpose: maintain competencies and keep up to date with current industry practices
- Especially relevant for persons who have NOT CONTINUOUSLY PERFORMED high risk work
- Person providing refresher training must be ENDORSED BY THE CRANE COMPANY as competent to provide it
- FREQUENCY: interval must NOT EXCEED 3 YEARS; training should occur BETWEEN 2.5 AND 3 YEARS after initial licence or most recent refresher (whichever is shorter)

REFRESHER TRAINING RECORD — must include:
- Person's full name and signature
- Relevant classes of HRWL held and licence numbers
- Name and signature of person conducting training
- Dates and times of training
- Details of training including type of equipment used/operated and outcomes achieved
- Verified and signed by PCBU or representative; PCBU keeps a copy

CONSTRUCTION INDUCTION (WHS Regulation s317):
- Workers must have successfully completed GENERAL CONSTRUCTION INDUCTION TRAINING before carrying out construction work
- If training completed more than 2 YEARS PREVIOUSLY: worker must have carried out construction work in the preceding 2 years

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operator starting work on unfamiliar crane type without documented familiarisation training = non-compliance
- No familiarisation training checklist on file for the specific crane model = flag
- Crane operator, dogger or rigger without refresher training within 3 years = non-compliance
- No evidence of construction induction (white card) for workers on construction site = non-compliance
- Refresher training record missing HRWL numbers or PCBU signature = incomplete records flag

CHUNK 35: Inspecting, Testing and Maintenance — Overview and Pre-Operational Inspection
Section: CoP s16; WHS Regulation s235
Topic: Competent person for inspections; pre-operational inspection checklist; routine maintenance

FAILURE TO MAINTAIN appropriate inspection and maintenance programs may lead to structural or mechanical failure and collapse.

LEVELS OF INSPECTION:
1. PRE-OPERATIONAL INSPECTION — before each work shift
2. ROUTINE INSPECTION AND MAINTENANCE — weekly, monthly, quarterly or by hours of operation
3. ANNUAL INSPECTIONS
4. MAJOR INSPECTIONS

COMPETENT PERSON — defined as:
- For MAJOR INSPECTIONS: person who has acquired knowledge/skills through training/qualification/experience to carry out major inspection AND is registered under a law providing for registration of professional engineers; OR is determined by regulator to be competent
- For OTHER CASES: person who has acquired knowledge and skills through training, qualification or experience to carry out the task

INSPECTING SPECIFIC PARTS:
- WELDS: competent person must have knowledge and experience in inspection and testing of welds; knowledge of NDT methods and AS/NZS 1554
- HYDRAULIC SYSTEMS: competent person must have knowledge and experience in inspection/testing of hydraulic systems
- ELECTRICAL SYSTEMS: must have knowledge and experience including ability to read circuit diagrams; must be QUALIFIED AND LICENSED ELECTRICIAN where voltage >50 VOLTS AC or >115 VOLTS DC
- NON-DESTRUCTIVE TESTING: competent person must be NATA ACCREDITED; technicians performing NDT independently must be AiNDT Cert II (or AiNDT Cert I with competent person providing procedure); NDT results must be available at crane yard

CRANE ALTERATIONS (WHS Regulation s244, s282):
- Altered design must be registered if alteration may affect health or safety
- Registration holders must notify WHS Regulator of changes requiring new control measures
- Proposed alterations must be assessed in relation to the WHOLE DEVICE; not in isolation
- Before alterations: consult designer and manufacturer; if unavailable: alterations by or under supervision of a REGISTERED PROFESSIONAL ENGINEER
- Before returning altered crane to service: implement control measures; inspect and test per altered design specifications

PRE-OPERATIONAL INSPECTION — before each work shift, crane operator must carry out visual inspection and functional test of:
- All relevant items in operator's manual
- Operating and emergency controls
- Brakes
- Safety switches and interlocks (including limiting and indicating devices)
- Visual inspection of structure
- Wire ropes: on drum and correctly reeved on sheave
- Results MUST BE ENTERED IN LOGBOOK accessible with the crane
- All safety-related problems must be CORRECTED BEFORE THE CRANE IS USED

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No logbook entries for pre-operational inspection before shift = non-compliance
- Crane in use with known safety-related problems not corrected = serious non-compliance
- NDT carried out by person without NATA accreditation = non-compliance
- Electrical systems inspection carried out by non-licensed electrician (voltage >50V AC) = non-compliance
- Crane alterations made without registered professional engineer supervision = non-compliance

CHUNK 36: Annual Inspections and Major Inspections
Section: CoP s16.5, s16.6; WHS Regulation s235
Topic: Annual inspection contents; major inspection trigger, scope, and key items; records requirements

ANNUAL INSPECTIONS — must include:
- All items specified by crane manufacturer for annual inspection
- All items from routine inspection and maintenance program
- Effective functioning and CALIBRATION of all limiting and indicating devices
- Detailed visual inspection and tolerance checking of all structural and wear components
- Checking tolerances for wear limits
- Detailed check for CORROSION
- Detailed examination of critical areas for evidence of CRACKING

ANNUAL INSPECTION CERTIFICATE: An example Annual Crane Safety Certificate is in Appendix 6 — may be used as evidence that the crane has received annual safety inspection by a competent person.

MAJOR INSPECTION — MANDATORY for registered mobile cranes (>10t):
Trigger — AT THE END OF THE CRANE'S DESIGN LIFE, or:
- If no manufacturer recommendations: per recommendations of a competent person
- If not reasonably practicable: at least EVERY 10 YEARS from date crane was first commissioned or first registered (whichever occurred first)

NON-REGISTRABLE cranes (≤10t): major inspection NOT mandatory but SHOULD be completed for continued safe operation.

MAJOR INSPECTION must be carried out by or under supervision of a REGISTERED PROFESSIONAL ENGINEER (or person determined by regulator to be competent).

KEY ITEMS REQUIRING MAJOR INSPECTION — at minimum:

SLEW RING:
- Clearance measured QUANTITATIVELY at MINIMUM OF 4 LOCATIONS around slew ring
- If clearance exceeds manufacturer's specification: slew ring must be SPLIT and all components examined
- Backlash and teeth width in pinion drives and ring drive: must be within manufacturer's specification

SLEW RING BOLTS — one of the following:
a. Remove all bolts and REPLACE WITH NEW BOLTS per manufacturer's specification and torque sequence
b. RE-TORQUE to manufacturer's specification (where manufacturer states this is acceptable)
c. Remove all bolts and NON-DESTRUCTIVELY TEST for cracking; reinstall undamaged bolts
d. Remove a PERCENTAGE of bolts per manufacturer's instructions; NDT removed bolts; if damage found, all bolts tested or replaced

DRIVE SYSTEMS (hydraulic motors, gearboxes, drive shafts):
- Where catastrophic failure can cause uncontrolled load or boom drop: REMOVE AND STRIP DOWN all parts; check all components within manufacturer's tolerance; replace worn components; NDT parts prone to cracking; hydraulic motors and valves: PRESSURE AND PERFORMANCE TESTED before re-entering service
- Where failure will NOT cause uncontrolled drop: inspect for vibration, fluid leakage and correct operation; repair/replace where damage or malfunction observed

BRAKING SYSTEMS:
- Hoist and luff brakes: REMOVED AND DISMANTLED; pins, springs, valves and bearings checked for tolerance; rubber seals replaced; pistons checked
- Welds in braking systems (including band brake weld terminations): CRACK TESTED BY NDT
- Hydraulic systems: checked for leaks before reattachment
- Brake linings: checked for wear limits
- After reinstallation: brakes ADJUSTED AND ACTUATED MULTIPLE TIMES to ensure correct operation

HYDRAULIC CYLINDERS (ALL cylinders including outrigger cylinders):
- Checked for external leakage and creep; if leakage or creep exceeds specifications: REMOVED AND STRIPPED DOWN
- Seals replaced; rams rechromed where necessary
- NDT crack test welds on rod ends and caps
- PRESSURE TESTED and checked for operation and leaks after reassembly

BOOMS (HYDRAULIC CRANES):
- CREEP TEST on telescopic function with boom at maximum extended and raised position; if creep exceeds specifications: disassemble and remove/repair/replace telescoping cylinder
- Check boom straightness in both planes to manufacturer's specifications; if exceeds tolerance: remove, dismantle, repair or replace
- Check wear pad clearance and replace worn pads
- Check for corrosion and damage; NDT accessible welds on boom sections and slew ring upper
- Pin type locks: inspect effective operation of locking mechanism

BOOMS (LATTICE BOOM CRANES):
- NDT for cracks on: all welds connecting male and female clevises (all boom sections); welds on boom butt section; welds on boom head; MINIMUM 10% OF LACING WELDS (all if cracks found)
- Chord thickness testing (ultrasonics) on all chords of boom sections
- Thickness testing of plate on butt section (near water drain holes)
- Check boom for straightness, damage and corrosion

RATED CAPACITY LIMITERS AND LOAD INDICATORS:
- Must be CHECKED AND CALIBRATED for correct operation
- Accuracy must be within tolerance per crane manufacturer or AS 1418.5
- CALIBRATION CERTIFICATE to be completed by competent person
- NOTE: RCLs and load indicators must be calibrated at MORE FREQUENT INTERVALS than the 10-year major inspection

STEEL WIRE ROPES:
- All hoist, luff, pendant, trolley and counterweight ropes: inspected for wear per discard criteria in AS 2759
- If rope likely to require replacement within 3 MONTHS: must be replaced immediately with rope passing AS 2759 inspection criteria
- Ropes must only be replaced with type SPECIFIED BY CRANE MANUFACTURER unless professional engineer specifies otherwise
- Pins and terminations on pendant ropes must also be inspected

ROPE SHEAVES:
- At major inspection: ALL ROPE SHEAVES removed from crane and closely inspected for condition and operation
- All sheaves must freely rotate without excessive end play
- Damaged and worn sheaves must be REPLACED
- SHEAVES MUST NOT BE PAINTED
- Information on identifying worn/degraded plastic rope sheaves should be requested from crane manufacturer

RECORDS OF INSPECTIONS AND MAINTENANCE:
- Crane service record (maintenance logbook) must be KEPT AND READILY AVAILABLE
- Must be easily understood; written in PLAIN ENGLISH
- Records may be in any suitable format; must be TRANSFERRED WITH OWNERSHIP OF CRANE
- Each entry must: clearly describe work and parts replaced; be dated; state name of person; be signed by person
- All complete routine, annual and major inspection reports must be maintained and available for examination

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No annual inspection certificate available for crane = non-compliance
- Major inspection overdue (crane >10t and 10+ years without major inspection) = serious non-compliance
- Major inspection carried out by person other than registered professional engineer = non-compliance
- Slew ring clearance not measured at minimum 4 locations = flag
- Rope sheaves painted = non-compliance
- Wire rope requiring replacement within 3 months not replaced = non-compliance
- No maintenance logbook readily available with the crane = non-compliance
- Maintenance records not signed by person carrying out work = incomplete records flag

CHUNK 37: Licencing — High Risk Work Licence Classes for Crane Operations
Section: CoP s11.1, s14.7, s15; WHS Regulation Schedule 3; Transport Operations (Road Use Management—Driver Licensing) Regulation 2021
Topic: Required HRWL classes for crane operators, doggers and riggers; driver licence requirements

HIGH RISK WORK LICENCES — required for crane operations:
- CRANE OPERATOR: must hold appropriate MOBILE CRANE LICENCE CLASS
- DOGGER: must hold HRWL for dogging work (WHS Regulation Schedule 3)
- VEHICLE-LOADING CRANE (≥10 metre tonnes load moment): must hold HRWL CLASS CV
- MULTIPLE CRANE LIFT SUPERVISOR: must hold HRWL in INTERMEDIATE or ADVANCED RIGGING
- DUAL CRANE LIFT BRIDGE BEAMS: INTERMEDIATE RIGGER minimum required to oversee

DRIVER LICENSING — required classes based on GVM (Transport Operations (Road Use Management—Driver Licensing) Regulation 2021):
- Mobile crane ≤8t GVM: CLASS LR (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM)
- Mobile crane >8t GVM, max 2 axles: CLASS MR (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM)
- Mobile crane >8t GVM, more than 2 axles: CLASS HR (with or without trailer ≤9t GVM)
- Mobile crane >8t GVM, more than 2 axles with trailer (no GVM restriction): CLASS HC
- All mobile cranes: CLASS MC (supersedes all lower classes)

IMPORTANT: Holding HRWL to operate a mobile crane does NOT authorise driving on a road. Both HRWL and heavy vehicle DRIVER LICENCE are required.

GENERAL CONSTRUCTION INDUCTION:
- All workers on construction sites must have completed general construction induction training (white card)
- If training completed more than 2 years previously: must have carried out construction work in the preceding 2 years

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Crane operator without valid HRWL for the specific crane type = serious non-compliance
- Dogger without valid dogging HRWL on site = serious non-compliance
- VLC operator with load moment ≥10mt without CV class HRWL = serious non-compliance
- Person supervising multiple crane lift without intermediate or advanced rigging HRWL = non-compliance
- Mobile crane driven on public road by person without appropriate heavy vehicle driver licence class = non-compliance
- Workers on construction site without evidence of general construction induction (white card) = non-compliance
