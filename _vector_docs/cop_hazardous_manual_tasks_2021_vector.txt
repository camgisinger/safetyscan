CHUNK 1: Repetitive and Sustained Task Thresholds
Section: CoP s2.1, s2.2, s3.3
Topic: Exact time and frequency measurements defining hazardous manual tasks (KEY MEASUREMENTS)

REPETITIVE MOVEMENT:
- Movement or force performed MORE THAN TWICE PER MINUTE = repetitive hazard

SUSTAINED POSTURE OR FORCE:
- Posture or force held for MORE THAN 30 SECONDS AT A TIME = sustained hazard

LONG DURATION TASK:
- Task done for MORE THAN 2 HOURS TOTAL OVER A WHOLE SHIFT, OR
- Task done CONTINUOUSLY FOR MORE THAN 30 MINUTES AT A TIME = long duration hazard

HIGH FORCE INDICATOR:
- Task requires TWO PEOPLE to complete safely = high force
- Task requires a stronger person than currently assigned = high force

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker performing same motion more than twice per minute without rotation = repetitive risk
- Worker holding heavy power tool unsupported for several minutes = sustained force risk
- Packaging line worker repeating same motion continuously = repetitive movement risk
- Two people assigned to handle a load = high force indicator
- Worker visibly grimacing or stopping to stretch during task = MSD risk sign

CHUNK 2: Awkward Posture Angle Thresholds
Section: CoP s3.3, Table 1; Appendix F
Topic: Exact degree measurements for awkward postures (KEY MEASUREMENTS)

BACK AND NECK BENDING:
- Bending back or head FORWARDS OR SIDEWAYS MORE THAN 20 DEGREES = awkward posture
- Bending back or head BACKWARDS MORE THAN 5 DEGREES = awkward posture
- Twisting back or neck MORE THAN 20 DEGREES = awkward posture

ARM AND REACH:
- Working with one or both hands ABOVE SHOULDER HEIGHT = awkward posture
- Reaching forwards or sideways MORE THAN 30cm FROM THE BODY = awkward posture

STANDING:
- Standing with most body weight on ONE LEG = awkward posture

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workstation height forcing worker to bend neck forward significantly = bending >20 degrees
- Storage shelves positioned so arms are constantly overhead = hand height risk
- Worker reaching across wide conveyor or workbench = reach >30cm risk
- Worker twisting torso repeatedly to place items = twisting >20 degrees risk
- Worker leaning forward significantly over work surface = back flexion >20 degrees
- Worker reaching behind body to grab components = awkward movement risk

CHUNK 3: Office Seating Specifications
Section: CoP s4.3, Table 2
Topic: Mandatory features for office chairs used in seated tasks (KEY MEASUREMENTS)

OFFICE SEATING MUST HAVE:
- Adjustable seat height AND angle
- Contoured backrest with lumbar curve
- Swivel action
- Five-point base with castors

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Office chair with only 4 legs = non-compliant equipment
- Chair with no height adjustment mechanism = non-compliant
- Chair with no swivel action = non-compliant
- Chair with flat backrest (no lumbar curve) = non-compliant
- Chair with fewer than 5 base points = non-compliant

CHUNK 4: Hand Tool Grip and Design Requirements
Section: CoP s4.4; Appendix B
Topic: Ergonomic tool specifications (KEY MEASUREMENTS)

HAND TOOL DESIGN REQUIREMENTS:
- Tool must be held in NEUTRAL WRIST OR HANDSHAKE POSITION
- Heaviest part of tool must be BEHIND THE WRIST
- Grip span of crimping/clamping tools: 10cm OR LESS

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker using pliers with straight handles forcing wrist into bent position = improper tool design
- Tool grip span visibly wider than 10cm on crimping/clamping tools = non-compliant tool
- Heavy power tool with weight forward of wrist = poor tool design
- Worker's wrist bent upward or downward while using tool = neutral position not maintained
- Hammer drills or grinders without vibration-reducing features = vibration hazard

CHUNK 5: Team Handling and Mechanical Aid Requirements
Section: CoP s4.4, s4.5
Topic: When team handling indicates task redesign is mandatory

TEAM HANDLING RULES:
- Team handling should only be used UNTIL a more effective control can be implemented OR for tasks undertaken RARELY
- If there is REGULAR NEED for team handling, the task MUST BE REDESIGNED to allow mechanical equipment use

MECHANICAL AIDS MUST BE PROVIDED where reasonably practicable:
- Examples: conveyors, overhead cranes, vacuum-assisted lifters, loading dock levellers, trolleys, hoists

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Two workers struggling to carry bulky load through narrow thoroughfare = high-risk team handling
- Regular team lifting occurring without mechanical alternative visible = must be redesigned
- Heavy rolls of material being lifted manually to high spindle = engineering control gap
- Mechanical aid (trolley, hoist) available but not being used = training/supervision failure
- Absence of mechanical aids for moving heavy pallets = hierarchy failure

CHUNK 6: Packaging and Load Labelling Requirements
Section: CoP s6.2; WHS Regulation s61
Topic: Mandatory features for packaged loads

PACKAGING MUST PROVIDE:
- TWO HANDLES to prevent one-handed lifting
- Label indicating HEAVIEST SIDE of off-centred load
- Label showing WEIGHT of load
- Label showing STABILITY characteristics of load

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Heavy cardboard boxes without reinforced handholds = supplier duty gap
- Large boxes with no weight labels = information provision failure
- Large boxes with no stability labels = non-compliant
- Boxes with only one handle or grip point = non-compliant packaging
- Large equipment without designated lifting points for cranes = design deficiency

CHUNK 7: Environmental Controls — Temperature and Lighting
Section: CoP s4.7
Topic: Visible environmental stressors affecting manual task safety

COLD ENVIRONMENT CONTROLS:
- PPE: gloves with good grip
- Heaters must be provided
- Rest breaks in warm place required

HEAT ENVIRONMENT CONTROLS:
- Fans or air-conditioning must be provided
- Cool drinking water must be supplied
- Screens to shield from radiant heat sources

LIGHTING REQUIREMENTS:
- Lighting must suit the task
- Diffusers or reflectors must prevent glare or shadows

FLOORS:
- Must be free of clutter or obstacles that force awkward postures

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers perspiring heavily while handling slippery loads = heat/humidity risk
- Inadequate lighting causing workers to lean closer to work = posture risk from environment
- Floors with clutter or obstacles forcing workers to reach over items = poor housekeeping and awkward posture risk
- No fans or air movement visible in hot environment = heat control gap
- Glare or shadows on work surface = lighting deficiency

---

CHUNK 8: Workstation Height and Adjustability
Section: CoP s4.3, Table 2
Topic: Height-adjustable workstations and workspace design

WORKSTATION DESIGN:
- Height-adjustable workstations must be provided where workers of different heights perform same task
- Workstations must allow neutral postures (no bending >20 degrees, no reaching >30cm)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workstations that cannot be adjusted to suit different worker heights = design failure
- No height-adjustable workstation where workers of different heights do same task = design gap
- Worker forced to bend forward significantly due to fixed workstation height = non-compliant design
- Worker standing on improvised platform (flattened cartons, blocks) to reach work height = design deficiency
- Work surface too high forcing arms above shoulder height = awkward posture risk

CHUNK 9: PPE as Last Resort Control
Section: CoP s4.1, s4.3, Table 2
Topic: PPE hierarchy position and examples

PPE IS THE LAST RESORT — only after elimination, substitution, engineering and administrative controls:
- Heat-resistant gloves (for thermal hazards)
- Shock-absorbent shoes for concrete floors
- Gloves with good grip (for cold environments)

PPE MUST NOT be the primary control for heavy lifting or high force tasks.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers relying on back belts as primary control for heavy lifting = inadequate hierarchy
- Workers wearing PPE (gloves, belts) but no mechanical aids available = hierarchy failure
- Back support belts visible on workers performing regular heavy lifting = control measure gap

---

CHUNK 10: Work Rate and Task Rotation
Section: CoP s4.5, s4.6
Topic: Workload management and rotation requirements

WORK RATE:
- Workers must NOT have to work at a rate that is at the limit of their ability

TASK ROTATION:
- Rotate workers between different tasks to reduce exposure duration
- Rotation is an administrative control (lower in hierarchy than engineering controls)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker unable to keep pace with process line speed = work rate exceeds capacity
- Same worker performing repetitive task for extended period without rotation = administrative control gap
- Worker showing signs of fatigue or strain during shift = work rate or rotation issue
- No evidence of task rotation where repetitive tasks exceed 2 hours total = administrative failure
