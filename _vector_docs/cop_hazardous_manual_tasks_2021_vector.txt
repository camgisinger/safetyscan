CHUNK 1: Definitions and Legal Duties
Section: COP s1.1, s1.2, s1.3; WHS Act s19
Topic: Defining hazardous tasks and primary safety obligations

MUSCULOSKELETAL DISORDER (MSD): An injury to, or a disease of, the musculoskeletal system, whether occurring suddenly or over time. Includes sprains, strains, back injuries, nerve compression (e.g. carpal tunnel), and chronic pain.

HAZARDOUS MANUAL TASK: A task requiring a person to lift, lower, push, pull, carry or otherwise move, hold or restrain any person, animal or thing involving one or more of:
- Repetitive or sustained force
- High or sudden force
- Repetitive movement
- Sustained or awkward posture
- Exposure to vibration

PRIMARY DUTY: A PCBU must manage risks to health and safety relating to a musculoskeletal disorder associated with a hazardous manual task. Must ELIMINATE risks, or if not reasonably practicable, MINIMISE the risks so far as is reasonably practicable.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker performing a task with jerky or unexpected movements = sudden force hazard
- Worker using improvisations (e.g. standing on flattened cartons to reach higher) = design/layout deficiency
- Two people assigned to handle a heavy load = high force indicator
- Worker visibly grimacing or stopping to stretch during repetitive task = MSD risk sign
- No mechanical aids available for heavy or bulky loads = control measure gap

CHUNK 2: Identification and Duration Thresholds
Section: COP s2.1, s2.2, s3.3
Topic: Exact measurements for repetitive and sustained hazards

KEY THRESHOLDS:
- REPETITIVE: A movement or force performed MORE THAN TWICE A MINUTE
- SUSTAINED: A posture or force held for MORE THAN 30 SECONDS AT A TIME
- LONG DURATION: Task done for MORE THAN A TOTAL OF 2 HOURS OVER A WHOLE SHIFT or CONTINUOUSLY FOR MORE THAN 30 MINUTES AT A TIME
- HIGH FORCE: Any task that a worker describes as very demanding physically, needs help to complete, or REQUIRES A STRONGER PERSON OR TWO PEOPLE TO COMPLETE

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker holding a heavy power tool (e.g. 3kg drill) unsupported for several minutes = sustained force risk
- Packaging tins from a fast-moving process line = repetitive movement risk (likely >2 times/minute)
- Worker showing signs of white finger or numbness = vibration exposure symptom
- Task clearly requiring two people being done by one = high force indicator
- Worker performing same motion repeatedly without rotation or rest = repetitive risk

CHUNK 3: Posture and Movement Risk Factors
Section: COP s3.3, Table 1; Appendix F
Topic: Exact degree thresholds for awkward postures

AWKWARD POSTURES — risk of MSD increases if the task involves:
- Bending the back or head FORWARDS OR SIDEWAYS MORE THAN 20 DEGREES
- Twisting the back or neck MORE THAN 20 DEGREES
- Bending the back or head BACKWARDS MORE THAN 5 DEGREES
- Working with one or both hands ABOVE SHOULDER HEIGHT
- Reaching forwards or sideways MORE THAN 30cm FROM THE BODY
- Standing with most of the body's weight on ONE LEG

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workstation height forcing a worker to bend neck forward significantly = bending >20 degrees
- Storage shelves positioned so high that arms are constantly overhead = hand height risk
- Worker reaching behind their body to grab components = awkward movement risk
- Worker twisting torso repeatedly to place items = twisting >20 degrees risk
- Worker leaning forward significantly over work surface = back flexion >20 degrees
- Worker reaching across a wide conveyor or workbench = reach >30cm risk

CHUNK 4: Control Measures — Hierarchy and Design
Section: COP s4.1, s4.3, Table 2
Topic: Mandatory order of controls and workspace requirements

HIERARCHY OF CONTROL (most to least preferred):
1. ELIMINATION: Automate the task (e.g. robotics) or deliver goods directly to point of use
2. SUBSTITUTION/ENGINEERING: Replace hand tools with power tools; use mechanical lifting aids; provide height-adjustable workstations; redesign the task or workplace
3. ADMINISTRATIVE: Rotate workers between different tasks; develop safe lifting procedures; limit exposure duration
4. PPE: Heat-resistant gloves; shock-absorbent shoes for concrete floors (last resort only)

OFFICE SEATING REQUIREMENTS — for seated tasks, seating should have:
- Adjustable seat height AND angle
- A contoured backrest with a lumbar curve
- A swivel action
- A five-point base with castors

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Absence of mechanical aids (trolleys, hoists) for moving heavy pallets = hierarchy failure
- Office chairs with only 4 legs or no height adjustment = non-compliant equipment
- Workstations that cannot be adjusted to suit different worker heights = design failure
- Workers relying on PPE (back belts) as the primary control for heavy lifting = inadequate hierarchy
- No height-adjustable workstation available where workers of different heights do the same task = design gap

CHUNK 5: Tools and Mechanical Aids
Section: COP s4.4; Appendix B
Topic: Ergonomic tool selection and mechanical support

TOOL DESIGN — hand tools should be designed and selected to:
- Be held in a NEUTRAL WRIST OR HANDSHAKE POSITION
- Be light and well-balanced, with the HEAVIEST PART OF THE TOOL BEHIND THE WRIST
- Have a GRIP SPAN OF 10cm OR LESS for crimping/clamping tools

MECHANICAL AIDS: PCBUs must ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, that workers are given adequate information, training, instruction and supervision when a mechanical aid is introduced.
- Examples: Conveyors, overhead cranes, vacuum-assisted lifters, loading dock levellers

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker using pliers with straight handles that force the wrist into a bent position = improper tool design
- Heavy rolls of material being lifted manually to a high spindle = engineering control gap (should use adjustable spindle or mechanical aid)
- Hammer drills or grinders being used without vibration-reducing features = vibration hazard
- Tool grip span visibly wider than 10cm on crimping/clamping tools = non-compliant tool
- Mechanical aid available but not being used = administrative/training failure

CHUNK 6: Systems of Work and Environment
Section: COP s4.5, s4.6, s4.7
Topic: Managing workloads, team handling, and environmental stressors

TEAM HANDLING:
- Should only be used until a more effective control can be implemented or for tasks undertaken rarely
- If there is a REGULAR NEED for team handling, the manual task MUST BE REDESIGNED to allow use of mechanical equipment

WORK RATE: Workers should not have to work at a rate that is at the limit of their ability.

ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROLS:
- Cold: Provide PPE (gloves with good grip) and heaters; ensure rest breaks in a warm place
- Heat: Provide fans/air-conditioning; supply cool drinking water; use screens to shield from radiant heat
- Lighting: Must suit the task; use diffusers or reflectors to prevent glare or shadows
- Floors: Must be free of clutter or obstacles that force workers to adopt awkward postures

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Two workers struggling to carry a bulky load through a narrow thoroughfare = high-risk team handling
- Regular team lifting occurring without any mechanical alternative in sight = must be redesigned
- Floors with clutter or obstacles forcing workers to reach over items = poor housekeeping and awkward posture risk
- Workers perspiring heavily while handling slippery loads = heat/humidity risk
- Inadequate lighting causing workers to lean closer to their work = posture risk from environment

CHUNK 7: Duties of Upstream Duty Holders
Section: COP s6.1, s6.2; WHS Regulation s61
Topic: Obligations for designers and manufacturers

DESIGN DUTY: A designer MUST ENSURE plant or structure is designed to ELIMINATE the need to carry out a hazardous manual task.

INFORMATION PROVISION: Manufacturers and designers must give each person provided with the design or plant adequate information about features that eliminate or minimise the need for hazardous manual tasks.

PACKAGING — manufacturers should consider:
- Providing TWO HANDLES to prevent one-handed lifting
- Labelling loads to indicate the HEAVIEST SIDE of an off-centred load
- Labelling the WEIGHT AND STABILITY of the load

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Heavy cardboard boxes arriving without reinforced handholds = supplier duty gap
- Large equipment arriving without designated lifting points for cranes = design deficiency
- Large boxes arriving with no weight or stability labels = information provision failure
- Machinery delivered without manual handling instructions or lifting point markings = non-compliant
