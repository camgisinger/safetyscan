CHUNK 1: Hazardous Noise Indicator — Voice Communication Test
Section: CoP s1.1, s2.2
Topic: Simple on-site test for identifying hazardous noise levels

VOICE COMMUNICATION TEST:
- If workers need to RAISE THEIR VOICES to communicate with someone ABOUT ONE METRE AWAY = hazardous noise likely present (indicative of >85 dB(A))
- This is a practical field indicator that noise assessment is required

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers shouting to communicate at 1m distance = hazardous noise likely present
- Workers using hand signals instead of voice communication = hazardous noise likely present
- Presence of "Hearing Protector Area" signs = identified hazardous noise zone — PPE mandatory in that area
- Workers not wearing provided hearing protection in signed areas = non-compliant
- No hearing protector signs in areas with obviously loud machinery = hazard not identified

---

CHUNK 2: Noise Measurement Position
Section: CoP s3.2, s4.3; AS/NZS 1269.1
Topic: Correct measurement location for noise assessment

MEASUREMENT POSITION:
- Noise measurements must be taken at 10 TO 20 CENTIMETRES from the worker's ear canal entrance
- Measurements should be taken WELL INTO THE WORK SHIFT to detect temporary hearing loss (not before shift starts)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Integrating sound level meter positioned 10-20cm from worker's ear during assessment = correct measurement practice
- Worker wearing a noise dosimeter clipped near shoulder/ear = positive measurement practice for mobile workers
- Sound level meter positioned far from worker's head = incorrect measurement
- Assessment conducted before work shift starts = may fail to detect temporary threshold shifts

---

CHUNK 3: Unprotected Exposure Time Limits — Exact Values
Section: CoP s2.2, Appendix D
Topic: Maximum permissible exposure times at specific noise levels without hearing protection

UNPROTECTED EXPOSURE TIME LIMITS (exact values):
- 85 dB(A): 8 hours maximum
- 88 dB(A): 4 hours maximum
- 91 dB(A): 2 hours maximum
- 94 dB(A): 1 hour maximum
- 100 dB(A): 15 minutes maximum
- 101 dB(A): 12 minutes maximum
- 121 dB(A): 7.2 seconds maximum
- LC,peak of 140 dB(C): NEVER PERMITTED without protection

CRITICAL PRINCIPLE:
- Every 3 dB increase in noise level can cause the same hearing damage in HALF THE TIME

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers exposed to obviously loud machinery (e.g. jackhammers, grinders, compressors) for extended periods without hearing protection = likely exceeding time limits
- No time tracking or rotation schedule visible for workers in high-noise areas = administrative control gap

---

CHUNK 4: Extended Shift Adjustments — Mandatory Additions
Section: CoP s4.3, Table 3
Topic: Noise limit adjustments for shifts longer than 8 hours

MANDATORY SHIFT ADJUSTMENTS — for shifts of 10 hours or longer, the following must be ADDED to the measured LAeq,8h before comparing to the 85 dB(A) standard:
- 10 hours to less than 14 hours: ADD +1 dB(A)
- 14 hours to less than 20 hours: ADD +2 dB(A)
- 20 hours or more: ADD +3 dB(A)

EXAMPLE: A 12-hour shift at measured 84 dB(A) = 84 + 1 = 85 dB(A) adjusted exposure = AT THE LIMIT

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Roster showing 12-hour shifts but noise records using flat 85 dB(A) limit without +1 dB adjustment = non-compliant risk assessment
- Night shift workers excluded from noise assessments = incomplete hazard identification
- Noise assessment records that do not record shift duration = potentially incomplete assessment

CHUNK 5: Compressed Air Silencers — Specific Noise Reduction
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Achievable noise reduction from compressed air exhaust silencers

COMPRESSED AIR SILENCERS:
- Silencers on compressed air exhausts: typically 10 TO 30 dB reduction
- One of the most effective and simple engineering controls

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Noisy compressed air exhausts without silencers = engineering control gap (10-30 dB reduction available)
- Compressed air tools venting directly to atmosphere = silencer opportunity not taken
- Silencers present but damaged or removed = control failure

---

CHUNK 6: Damping Material Coverage — Exact Reduction Values
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Noise reduction achievable by covering flat surfaces with damping material

DAMPING MATERIAL APPLICATION:
- Covering 80 PER CENT of a flat surface with damping material = 5 TO 25 dB noise reduction
- Applies to metal panels, guards, hoppers, chutes, bins

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Large flat metal surfaces (hoppers, chutes, bins, guards) without damping material = engineering control opportunity (5-25 dB reduction available)
- Damping material applied to less than 80% of surface = reduced effectiveness
- Damping material peeling or missing = control degradation

---

CHUNK 7: Machine Guard Gaps — Exact Reduction Values
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Noise reduction achievable by reducing gaps in machine guards

MACHINE GUARD GAP REDUCTION:
- Reducing gap area by HALF reduces noise by 3 dB
- Reducing gap area by 90% reduces noise by 10 dB
- Acoustic absorbent lining inside machine guards = additional reduction

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Machine guards with large gaps or openings = noise control failure (closing gaps gives 3-10 dB reduction)
- Guards with acoustic absorbent lining on inside = positive engineering design indicator
- Guards removed or left open during operation = control failure

---

CHUNK 8: Chain Drive vs Timing Belt Substitution
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Noise reduction from substituting chain drives with timing belts

SUBSTITUTION — CHAIN TO TIMING BELT:
- Replacing chain drives with timing belts (chevron pattern): 6 TO 20 dB noise reduction
- WELDING IS GENERALLY QUIETER THAN RIVETING

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Noisy chain drives visible where timing belts could be substituted = substitution opportunity not taken (6-20 dB reduction available)
- Riveting operations where welding could be substituted = quieter alternative available

---

CHUNK 9: Pipeline Flow Speed Limit
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F; s6.1
Topic: Maximum flow speed to minimize pipeline noise

PIPELINE FLOW SPEED:
- Keep to MAXIMUM 5 m/sec for low noise in pipelines

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- High-velocity flow noise audible from pipework = may exceed 5 m/sec limit
- No flow speed data available for noisy pipeline systems = design control gap

---

CHUNK 10: Vibration Mountings for Heavy Equipment
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Engineering control for structure-borne noise from heavy machinery

VIBRATION MOUNTINGS:
- Vibration mountings under heavy, noisy equipment: reduces structure-borne noise transmission
- Applies to compressors, pumps, generators, heavy presses

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Heavy machinery (compressors, pumps, generators) mounted directly to floor without vibration isolators = engineering control gap
- Vibration mountings present but damaged or compressed flat = control failure

---

CHUNK 11: Exhaust Mufflers on Internal Combustion Engines
Section: CoP s5.1, Appendix F
Topic: Mandatory noise control for engines

EXHAUST MUFFLERS:
- Exhaust mufflers on internal combustion engines: significant noise reduction
- Mandatory on most mobile plant and generators

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Internal combustion engine (generator, compressor, vehicle) without exhaust muffler = serious control failure
- Muffler removed, damaged or bypassed = control failure
- Straight-pipe exhaust on mobile plant = non-compliant

CHUNK 12: Hearing Protector Class Selection — Exact Requirements
Section: CoP s5.3, Table 4
Topic: Minimum hearing protector class required for specific noise levels

HEARING PROTECTOR CLASS SELECTION (Table 4) — minimum class required:
- Less than 90 dB(A): Class 1
- 90 to less than 95 dB(A): Class 2
- 95 to less than 100 dB(A): Class 3
- 100 to less than 105 dB(A): Class 4
- 105 to less than 110 dB(A): Class 5

IMPULSE NOISE ABOVE 140 dB(C):
- CLASS 5 protectors SHALL BE USED for impulse noise from impacts or tools
- For blasting: WELL-FITTED EARPLUGS (at least Class 3) IN COMBINATION WITH EAR-MUFFS OF ANY CLASSIFICATION shall be worn

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker using Class 1 earplugs in area marked >100 dB(A) = dangerous under-protection
- Worker using only earplugs (no ear-muffs) during blasting = non-compliant for impulse noise
- Hearing protector packaging visible showing class rating lower than required for area = incorrect PPE selection

---

CHUNK 13: Hearing Protector Effectiveness Gap — Critical Limitation
Section: CoP s5.3
Topic: Dramatic loss of protection from brief removal of hearing protectors

EFFECTIVENESS GAP — critical limitation of PPE:
- One hour WITHOUT wearing a hearing protector during an 8-hour shift causes the MAXIMUM PROTECTION LEVEL TO FALL FROM 30 dB TO ONLY 9 dB
- Hearing protectors only work when worn — continuous correct use is essential

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker removing hearing protectors during noisy work even briefly = significant protection loss
- Hearing protectors worn around neck or on hard hat instead of over ears = zero protection
- Workers taking "breaks" from wearing protectors in noisy areas = effectiveness failure

---

CHUNK 14: Hearing Protector Fit and Maintenance
Section: CoP s5.3
Topic: Physical conditions that reduce hearing protector effectiveness

EAR-MUFF FIT REQUIREMENTS:
- Ear-muff seals must be intact, not cracked or leaking
- Workers wearing spectacles with ear-muffs: spectacle arms break the seal = PPE effectiveness failure
- Damaged or deformed ear-muff cushions must be replaced immediately

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Ear-muff seals that are damaged, cracked, or leaking = maintenance failure; replace immediately
- Workers wearing spectacles with ear-muffs that do not provide a close fit = PPE effectiveness failure
- Ear-muff cushions visibly compressed, hardened or torn = replace immediately
- Dirty or contaminated ear-muff cushions = hygiene and effectiveness issue

CHUNK 15: Audiometric Testing Timeframes
Section: CoP s5.4, s4.3; AS/NZS 1269.4
Topic: Mandatory medical monitoring intervals for noise-exposed workers

AUDIOMETRIC TESTING REQUIREMENTS:
- Baseline (initial) test: WITHIN THREE MONTHS of the worker commencing work
- Regular follow-up tests: AT LEAST EVERY TWO YEARS
- High exposure (≥100 dB(A)): More frequent testing may be needed
- Tests should be undertaken WELL INTO THE WORK SHIFT to detect temporary hearing loss (not before shift starts)

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- PCBU unable to produce baseline audiograms for workers hired more than 3 months ago = non-compliant monitoring
- No audiometric records for workers in high-noise areas = non-compliant
- Audiometric testing performed before work shift starts = may fail to detect temporary threshold shifts
- Workers reporting tinnitus (ringing in ears) without subsequent control measure review = regulatory breach

CHUNK 16: Ototoxic Chemical Exposure — Reduced Noise Limit
Section: CoP s2.1, Appendix B, Table 6
Topic: Lower noise exposure limit for workers exposed to hearing-damaging chemicals

OTOTOXIC SUBSTANCES — chemicals that damage hearing and increase susceptibility to noise-induced hearing loss:
- Solvents: Toluene, Xylene, Styrene
- Heavy metals: Lead, Mercury
- Asphyxiants: Carbon Monoxide

REDUCED NOISE LIMIT FOR OTOTOXIN EXPOSURE:
- Noise exposure SHOULD BE REDUCED TO 80 dB(A) OR BELOW (lower than the standard 85 dB(A))

AUDIOMETRIC TRIGGER FOR OTOTOXINS:
- Audiometric testing recommended if airborne exposure to ototoxins is GREATER THAN 50 PER CENT of the workplace exposure standard, REGARDLESS OF THE NOISE LEVEL

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers painting or degreasing with solvents in a noisy area without noise levels reduced to 80 dB(A) = high-risk interaction gap
- Solvent use (toluene, xylene, styrene) in loud workshop without audiometric monitoring even if noise is below 85 dB(A) = flag if solvent exposure >50% of WES
- Lead or mercury exposure combined with noise exposure without enhanced controls = combined hazard requiring lower noise limit

---

CHUNK 17: Combined Physical Hazards — Vibration and Noise
Section: CoP s2.1, Appendix B
Topic: Increased hearing loss risk from combined vibration and noise exposure

COMBINED PHYSICAL HAZARDS:
- Workers using jackhammers (hand-arm vibration) or operating earthmoving plant (whole-body vibration) while exposed to noise = increased susceptibility to hearing loss
- Vibration exposure makes workers MORE SUSCEPTIBLE to noise-induced hearing damage

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Jackhammer or grinder use (hand-arm vibration tools) by workers also exposed to high noise = combined hazard requiring enhanced controls
- Earthmoving plant operators (whole-body vibration) without cab noise assessments = combined hazard gap
- Workers using vibrating tools in noisy areas without enhanced hearing protection = high-risk interaction

---

CHUNK 18: Manufacturer Noise Emission Data Requirements
Section: WHS Regulation s59; CoP s6.1, s6.3
Topic: Information that must be provided with plant and equipment

INFORMATION THAT MUST BE PROVIDED by designers and manufacturers:
- NOISE EMISSION VALUES (sound power level or sound pressure level in dB(A))
- OPERATING CONDITIONS of the plant when noise was measured
- METHODS USED to measure the noise emission

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Machine without noise emission data available on site = manufacturer information duty gap
- Equipment nameplate or manual showing noise emission values = positive compliance indicator
- New plant installed without noise data provided to PCBU = designer/manufacturer duty breach

CHUNK 19: Hierarchy of Control — Visual Compliance Indicators
Section: CoP s5.1
Topic: Observable evidence of control hierarchy application

HIERARCHY COMPLIANCE — VISUAL EVIDENCE:
- LEVEL 2 (Engineering controls present):
  - Compressed air exhausts WITH silencers fitted
  - Heavy machinery WITH vibration mountings
  - Machine guards WITH acoustic lining inside
  - Timing belts used instead of chain drives
  - Exhaust mufflers fitted to engines

- LEVEL 4 (PPE as last resort):
  - Noisy areas relying solely on hearing protectors with no engineering controls in place = hierarchy violation
  - Engineering control opportunities visible but not implemented = hierarchy violation

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Noisy compressed air exhausts without silencers = engineering control gap (10-30 dB reduction available)
- Heavy machinery without vibration mountings = engineering control gap
- Absence of machine guards or guards with large gaps = noise control failure
- Noisy areas relying solely on hearing protectors with no engineering controls = hierarchy violation
- Chain drives visible where timing belts could be substituted = substitution opportunity not taken
