CHUNK 1: Abrasive Blasting — Definition and Duties
Section: COP s1.1, s1.2; WHS Act s19, s28
Topic: What constitutes abrasive blasting and who holds safety responsibilities

ABRASIVE BLASTING means propelling a stream of abrasive material at high speed against a surface using compressed air, liquid, steam, centrifugal wheels, or paddles to clean, abrade, etch, or change the original appearance/condition of the surface.

COMMON METHODS:
- Manual: Compressed air propelling material from a blast pot through a hose to a nozzle
- Automated: Centrifugal wheel systems and tumblers
- Environments: Enclosed blasting chambers/cabinets or open sites (bridges, tanks, boats)

DUTY HOLDERS:
- PCBU: Primary duty to eliminate or minimise risks; must consult workers
- Designers/Manufacturers/Suppliers: Ensure plant/substances are without risks; provide testing and specific information
- Officers: Exercise due diligence to ensure PCBU compliance
- Workers: Take reasonable care for own safety; comply with instructions and wear provided PPE

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Abrasive material being propelled at a surface = abrasive blasting activity identified
- Worker performing blasting without site-specific induction/training = non-compliant
- Presence of "Dead man control" on the nozzle = positive safety indicator

CHUNK 2: Prohibited and Restricted Chemicals
Section: WHS Regulation s382, Schedule 10; COP s3.1
Topic: Mandatory prohibitions on hazardous materials used for blasting

PROHIBITED MATERIALS — a PCBU MUST NOT use or allow a worker to use substances containing greater than the following concentrations for abrasive blasting:
- Crystalline Silicon Dioxide (Free Silica): >1%
- Lead: >0.1% (or levels exposing operator above regulatory limits)
- Arsenic, Antimony, Beryllium, Cadmium, Cobalt, Nickel, Tin: >0.1%
- Chromium: >0.5% (except for specified wet blasting)
- Asbestos: Use of high-pressure water or compressed air on asbestos-containing materials is STRICTLY PROHIBITED

WET BLASTING RESTRICTIONS: Must not use substances containing chromate, nitrate, or nitrite.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Use of river sand, beach sand, or white sand = ILLEGAL (contains >1% free silica)
- Safety Data Sheet (SDS) showing >0.1% lead for blast media = PROHIBITED
- Blasting on suspected asbestos without professional analysis = high-risk non-compliance

CHUNK 3: Dust Hazards — Silica and Lead
Section: COP s3.2; WHS Regulation s49, s392
Topic: Managing toxic dusts generated during the blasting process

CRYSTALLINE SILICA: Generated by blasting surfaces like concrete, sandstone, or using media like garnet with trace silica. Causes SILICOSIS (irreversible lung scarring).

LEAD DUST: Generated by blasting lead-based paint (common on bridges, ships, old housing). Absorption via inhalation or ingestion causes LEAD POISONING.

LEAD PROCESS DEFINITION: Includes using power tools or blasting to remove paint containing >1% lead by dry weight.
- Requires notifying workers of health risks before engagement
- Requires determining if work is "Lead Risk Work" based on blood lead levels

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Visible clouds of dust escaping a work area = ineffective containment/ventilation
- Workers eating or smoking in the blast area = high risk of lead ingestion
- Dry sweeping of spent abrasive = non-compliant (use vacuum or wet methods)

CHUNK 4: Engineering Controls — Cabinets and Chambers
Section: COP s3.2, s3.4
Topic: Requirements for blasting enclosures and ventilation

BLASTING CABINETS (Small Objects):
- Fully sealed; operator works from outside via sealed windows/gloves
- Must have interlocked doors to prevent operation while open
- Respiratory protection generally not required if maintained

BLASTING CHAMBERS (Large Objects) — VENTILATION SPEEDS:
- Down-draught: Minimum 0.3 m/s
- Cross-draught: Minimum 0.4 m/s

CHAMBER EMERGENCY EXIT:
- Must be at the furthest position from the main entrance
- Must be signposted and backlit

CHAMBER ILLUMINATION: At least 200 lux

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Dust leaking from cabinet door seals = maintenance failure
- Operator opening chamber doors immediately after blasting = non-compliant (allow 5-minute extraction time)
- Chamber emergency exit blocked or not illuminated = serious safety breach

CHUNK 5: Site Controls — Exclusion Zones and Temporary Enclosures
Section: COP s3.2
Topic: Managing open-air or large-scale blasting risks

EXCLUSION ZONES: Used to protect unprotected persons from dust and noise.
- Size determined by risk assessment (take wind direction into account)
- Must be marked with warning signs: "DANGER – ABRASIVE BLASTING IN PROGRESS"

TEMPORARY ENCLOSURES (for fixed structures such as bridges and tanks):
- Screens should extend 2 metres ABOVE the structure
- Porous materials (e.g. shade cloth) MUST NOT be used for toxic dusts like silica or lead

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Warning signs "DANGER – ABRASIVE BLASTING IN PROGRESS" visible = compliant
- Abrasive blasting in windy conditions without extra containment = non-compliant
- Spent abrasive piling up on site = poor housekeeping; risk of drift
- No exclusion zone marked = non-compliant

CHUNK 6: Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
Section: COP s3.2; WHS Regulation s44
Topic: Mandatory gear for blasting operators

RESPIRATORY PROTECTION:
- Airline respirator: Positive pressure hood/helmet type with inner bib and high-visibility shoulder cape
- Air quality must meet AS/NZS 1715; air-fed respirators should have carbon monoxide alarms
- Pot attendants: Must wear at least an air-purifying respirator while blasting is in progress

BODY PROTECTION:
- Protective suits: Leather or elastic straps at wrists/ankles with overlapping flaps
- Gloves: Industrial safety gloves (canvas or leather) per AS/NZS 2161
- Footwear: Waterproof material that minimises particle penetration

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker using a standard dust mask for manual blasting = LIFE-THREATENING NON-COMPLIANCE
- Helmet being hung by the air feed hose = potential damage to equipment
- Damaged or taped-up holes in protective suits = non-compliant body protection
- Pot attendant without respirator during blasting = non-compliant

CHUNK 7: Plant and Equipment Safety
Section: COP s3.3, s3.4
Topic: Mechanical safety, Dead man controls, and hoses

DEAD MAN CONTROL — a fast-acting self-actuating cut-off device under direct control of the nozzle operator:
- MANDATORY on all blast nozzles
- Must NEVER be taped down or modified
- Pneumatic type: suitable up to 40m
- Electric type: required over 40m for instant response

BLAST HOSES AND COUPLINGS:
- Must be anti-static or fitted with an earth wire to prevent static shock/explosion
- Hose whip checks MUST be fitted to all couplings to prevent injury if a connection fails
- Rated working pressure must not be exceeded
- Visible pin holes in hoses = imminent blow-out risk — must be replaced immediately

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Nozzle operator dropping the hose and flow continuing = Dead man control failure or tampering
- Blast hose with visible pin holes taped over = imminent blow-out risk
- Hose couplings without safety locks/pins = non-compliant

CHUNK 8: Noise, Heat, and Vibration
Section: COP s4.1, s4.2, s4.3; WHS Regulation s57
Topic: Managing physical environmental hazards

NOISE:
- Blasting nozzles generate 112–119 dB(A)
- Exposure standard: 85 dB(A) over 8 hours
- Unprotected exposure at 101 dB(A) is limited to 12 MINUTES per shift

HEAT: Risk of heat strain from PPE and confined/poorly ventilated spaces.
- Control: Fit cooling devices to blast helmet air supplies
- Provide cool drinking water

VIBRATION: Prolonged use causes Occupational Raynaud's disease (white finger).
- Control: Vibration-isolating handles and job rotation

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Operator not wearing hearing protection inside a blast helmet = non-compliant
- Operator showing white/numb fingers = symptom of vibration injury
- No cool drinking water provided on a hot blasting site = non-compliant

CHUNK 9: Health Monitoring and Records
Section: WHS Regulation s368; COP s2.4
Topic: Medical tracking for workers exposed to hazardous chemicals

MANDATORY HEALTH MONITORING required if there is a significant risk to health from exposure to:
- Crystalline Silica
- Inorganic Lead
- Cadmium, Asbestos, Arsenic, Chromium

RECORD KEEPING:
- Health monitoring reports: must be kept for 30 YEARS (40 years for asbestos)
- Air monitoring results: must be kept for 30 YEARS
- Training records: must be kept to demonstrate competency

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker performing lead-risk blasting without a baseline blood test = non-compliant
- PCBUs unable to produce air monitoring records for silica-intensive work = flag
