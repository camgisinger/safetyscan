CHUNK 1: Hierarchy of Controls — Mandatory Priority Order
Section: s4, s4.1; Figure 2; WHS Regulation s36
Topic: Required order for selecting control measures from most to least effective

LEVEL 1 — ELIMINATION (most effective):
- Remove the hazard completely
- Example: Work at ground level instead of height; remove chemical from process

LEVEL 2 — SUBSTITUTION, ISOLATION, ENGINEERING:
- SUBSTITUTION: Replace with less hazardous (water-based paint vs solvent; quieter equipment)
- ISOLATION: Physical separation (guardrails, barriers, exclusion zones, enclosures)
- ENGINEERING: Physical controls built in (mechanical hoists, machine guards, safety switches, local exhaust ventilation)

LEVEL 3 — ADMINISTRATIVE:
- Procedures, training, supervision, job rotation, permit systems, warning signs
- Less reliable — depends on human behaviour

LEVEL 4 — PPE (least effective):
- Hard hats, gloves, safety glasses, hearing protection, respirators, harnesses
- Must NOT be sole control if higher-order controls are practicable

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- PPE only for noisy machine when sound enclosure is practicable = hierarchy violation
- Safety signs only at fall edge instead of physical guardrail = non-compliant
- Guardrails or machine guards removed or bypassed = Level 2 failure
- Workers relying on "don't go near edge" with no physical barrier = inadequate control
- PPE visibly wrong type, damaged, or poor fit = Level 4 failure

CHUNK 2: Hazard Identification — Common Categories
Section: s2, s2.1; Table 1
Topic: Workplace hazard types that must be identified

COMMON HAZARD CATEGORIES:
- Manual tasks: Sustained/awkward postures, high/sudden force, repetition, vibration
- Gravity: Falling objects, falls from height, slips, trips
- Psychosocial: Time pressure, bullying, violence, fatigue
- Machinery/equipment: Hit by vehicles, caught in moving parts, plant failure
- Hazardous chemicals: Acids, hydrocarbons, asbestos, silica, flammables
- Extreme temperatures: Burns, heat stroke, hypothermia, frostbite
- Electricity: Contact with energised parts, arc flash, shock
- Noise/vibration: Hearing loss, hand-arm vibration syndrome
- Biological: Infectious diseases, contaminated materials

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker standing on boxes to reach = design/hazard identification gap
- Cluttered walkways or blocked emergency exits = gravity/trip hazard uncontrolled
- Equipment with worn insulation or visible defects = new hazard not assessed
- Worker improvisations to avoid discomfort = hazard identification failure

CHUNK 3: Risk Assessment — Severity and Likelihood
Section: s3, s3.2; Table 2
Topic: Evaluating how severe and likely harm is

SEVERITY FACTORS:
- Chain reaction potential (one failure leads to others)
- Escalation potential (small event becomes large — fire near combustibles)
- Number of people who could be harmed
- Vulnerability of people (new workers, young workers)

LIKELIHOOD RATINGS:
- Certain: Expected in most circumstances
- Very likely: Will probably occur in most circumstances
- Possible: Might occur occasionally
- Unlikely: Could happen at some time
- Rare: Only in exceptional circumstances

FACTORS INCREASING LIKELIHOOD:
- Worker fatigue during high-demand periods
- Environmental changes without updated controls (rain, heat)
- Inexperienced/new workers on complex tasks without close supervision
- Distractions or time pressure

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Visible worker fatigue during high-demand periods = increased error likelihood
- Wet/slippery surfaces without updated controls = assessment gap
- New/young workers on high-risk tasks without supervisor present = high-risk variability
- No risk assessment records for known hazards = non-compliant

CHUNK 4: Control Review Triggers
Section: s5; WHS Regulation s38
Topic: Mandatory situations requiring review of safety controls

REVIEW REQUIRED WHEN:
- Control measure NOT EFFECTIVE in controlling risk
- BEFORE A CHANGE at workplace creating new/different risk
- NEW HAZARD OR RISK identified
- CONSULTATION results indicate review necessary
- HEALTH AND SAFETY REPRESENTATIVE requests review
- After INCIDENT or near miss

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Control creating new problems (hose becomes trip hazard) = review required immediately
- Scheduled review date on risk register already passed = compliance failure
- Incident reports showing same injury recurring = controls not effective; review mandatory
- New plant/equipment/chemicals without updated risk assessment = review not conducted

CHUNK 5: Documentation and Record Keeping
Section: s6
Topic: Required records for demonstrating compliance

USEFUL RECORDS:
- Identified hazards and chosen controls
- How/when controls implemented and monitored
- Who was consulted
- Training records (date, topic, attendees, signatures)

MANDATORY:
- SWMS must be available AT LOCATION of high-risk construction work — not just site office
- Some hazard records (chemicals, plant) must be kept for time specified in WHS Regulation

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- PCBU unable to produce risk register during inspection = serious documentation gap
- No training records (date, topic, signatures) for safety procedure = record-keeping failure
- SWMS not available at location of high-risk work = regulatory breach
- Risk register with no review dates or responsible persons = inadequate documentation
- Hazard identification forms blank/incomplete = process not followed

CHUNK 6: Mandatory Risk Assessments
Section: s1.2; WHS Regulation s34–38
Topic: Activities requiring formal documented risk assessment

MANDATORY RISK ASSESSMENT REQUIRED FOR:
- Entry into CONFINED SPACES
- DIVING WORK
- LIVE ELECTRICAL WORK
- Other activities specified in WHS Regulation

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- No documented risk assessment for confined space entry = serious non-compliance
- Workers entering confined space without visible permit/assessment = regulatory breach
- Live electrical work without documented risk assessment = non-compliant

CHUNK 7: Reasonably Practicable — Cost vs Risk
Section: s1.1, s1.2; WHS Act s18, s19
Topic: Factors determining control feasibility

DETERMINING "REASONABLY PRACTICABLE" — weigh ALL factors:
- LIKELIHOOD of hazard/risk occurring
- DEGREE OF HARM that might result
- KNOWLEDGE about hazard/risk and ways to eliminate/minimise
- AVAILABILITY AND SUITABILITY of ways to eliminate/minimise
- COST — including whether GROSSLY DISPROPORTIONATE to risk

IMPORTANT:
- Cost CANNOT justify doing nothing
- Cost CANNOT justify behaviour-only controls when engineering/isolation is practicable

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers in obviously hazardous ways without documented oversight = duty breach
- More effective control bypassed for cheaper, less reliable option = reasonably practicable failure
- Safety equipment present but not in use = supervision/worker duty failure
- PPE provided but no higher-order controls where practicable = hierarchy failure
