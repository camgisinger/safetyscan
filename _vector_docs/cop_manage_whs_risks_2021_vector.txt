CHUNK 1: Primary Duties and Reasonably Practicable
Section: s1.1, s1.2; WHS Act s18, s19
Topic: Core legal obligations and factors for determining control feasibility

PRIMARY DUTY: A PCBU must ELIMINATE risks in the workplace, or if that is not reasonably practicable, MINIMISE the risks so far as is reasonably practicable.

DETERMINING "REASONABLY PRACTICABLE" — requires taking into account and weighing up ALL of the following:
- The LIKELIHOOD of the hazard or risk occurring
- The DEGREE OF HARM that might result from the hazard or risk
- KNOWLEDGE about the hazard or risk, and ways of eliminating or minimising it
- The AVAILABILITY AND SUITABILITY of ways to eliminate or minimise the risk
- The COST associated with available ways of eliminating or minimising the risk, including whether the cost is GROSSLY DISPROPORTIONATE to the risk

IMPORTANT: Cost CANNOT be used as a reason for doing nothing. It also CANNOT be used as a reason for adopting controls that rely exclusively on changing people's behaviour when more effective controls (engineering or isolation) are available and practicable.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Workers performing tasks in obviously hazardous ways without documented oversight = duty breach
- A more effective control measure bypassed for a cheaper, less reliable option = reasonably practicable failure
- Safety equipment present on site but not in use = supervision or worker duty failure
- PPE provided but no higher-order controls implemented where they are practicable = hierarchy failure

CHUNK 2: The Four-Step Risk Management Process
Section: s1.2; Figure 1; WHS Regulation s34–38
Topic: The systematic process all PCBUs must follow

A PCBU must manage risks to health and safety by following this systematic process:
1. IDENTIFY HAZARDS — find out what could cause harm
2. ASSESS RISKS (if necessary) — understand the nature of the harm, its severity, and likelihood
3. CONTROL RISKS — implement the MOST EFFECTIVE control measure that is reasonably practicable and ensure it remains effective over time
4. REVIEW control measures to ensure they are working as planned

MANDATORY RISK ASSESSMENTS — a risk assessment is mandatory under the WHS Regulation for:
- Entry into CONFINED SPACES
- DIVING WORK
- LIVE ELECTRICAL WORK
- Other activities as specified in the WHS Regulation

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Risk register or hazard log visible on site = positive indicator of Steps 1 and 2
- Safety meeting minutes or toolbox talk records = evidence of consultation in the process
- Uncontrolled hazards (e.g. a spill, unguarded edge) left unattended = process failure
- SWMS available at location of high-risk work = positive compliance indicator
- No documented risk assessment for confined space entry = serious non-compliance

CHUNK 3: Step 1 — Hazard Identification
Section: s2, s2.1; Table 1
Topic: Finding hazards and common workplace categories

IDENTIFICATION DUTY: A duty holder must identify REASONABLY FORESEEABLE HAZARDS that could give rise to risk. Hazards generally arise from:
- The PHYSICAL WORK ENVIRONMENT
- EQUIPMENT and SUBSTANCES used
- WORK TASKS performed
- WORK DESIGN and MANAGEMENT

COMMON HAZARD CATEGORIES (Table 1):
- Manual tasks: Sustained or awkward postures, high or sudden force, repetition, vibration
- Gravity: Falling objects, falls from height, slips and trips
- Psychosocial: Excessive time pressure, bullying, violence, work-related fatigue
- Machinery and equipment: Being hit by vehicles, caught in moving parts, plant failure
- Hazardous chemicals: Acids, hydrocarbons, asbestos, silica, flammable substances
- Extreme temperatures: Heat (burns, heat stroke) and cold (hypothermia, frostbite)
- Electricity: Contact with energised parts, arc flash, electric shock
- Noise and vibration: Hearing loss, hand-arm vibration syndrome
- Biological hazards: Infectious diseases, contaminated materials

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Worker improvisations to avoid discomfort (e.g. standing on boxes to reach) = design or hazard identification gap
- Cluttered walkways or obstructed emergency exits = gravity/trip hazard not controlled
- Hired or used equipment with worn insulation or visible defects = new hazard introduced to workplace without assessment
- No hazard identification process visible on site = PCBU duty gap

CHUNK 4: Step 2 — Risk Assessment
Section: s3, s3.2; Table 2
Topic: Evaluating severity and likelihood of harm

PURPOSE OF RISK ASSESSMENT: To determine how severe a risk is, the effectiveness of existing controls, and how urgently action is required.

SEVERITY FACTORS to consider:
- Whether one failure could lead to other failures (chain reaction)
- Whether a small event could ESCALATE TO A MUCH LARGER EVENT (e.g. a minor fire near combustible materials)
- The number of people who could be harmed
- The vulnerability of people at risk (e.g. new workers, young workers)

LIKELIHOOD RATINGS:
- Certain to occur: Expected in most circumstances
- Very likely: Will probably occur in most circumstances
- Possible: Might occur occasionally
- Unlikely: Could happen at some time
- Rare: Only in exceptional circumstances

FACTORS THAT INCREASE LIKELIHOOD:
- Worker fatigue during high-demand periods
- Changes in environmental conditions without updated controls (e.g. rain, heat)
- Inexperienced, young, or new workers performing complex tasks without close supervision
- Distractions or time pressure in the work environment

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Visible worker fatigue during high-demand periods = increased likelihood of human error
- Wet or slippery surfaces without updated controls = assessment gap
- New or young workers performing high-risk tasks without a supervisor present = high-risk individual variability
- No risk assessment records for activities with known hazards = non-compliant

CHUNK 5: Step 3 — Hierarchy of Controls
Section: s4, s4.1; Figure 2; WHS Regulation s36
Topic: Mandatory priority order for controlling risks

HIERARCHY OF CONTROL — must be applied in this order (highest to lowest effectiveness):

LEVEL 1 — ELIMINATION (most effective):
- The most effective control measure — remove the hazard completely
- Must ALWAYS be considered first
- Example: Doing work at ground level to eliminate fall risk; removing a chemical from the process

LEVEL 2 — SUBSTITUTION, ISOLATION, AND ENGINEERING:
- SUBSTITUTION: Replace with something less hazardous (e.g. water-based instead of solvent paints; quieter equipment)
- ISOLATION: Physically separate people from the hazard (e.g. guardrails, barriers, exclusion zones, enclosures)
- ENGINEERING CONTROLS: Physical controls built into the workplace or equipment (e.g. mechanical hoists, machine guards, safety switches, local exhaust ventilation)

LEVEL 3 — ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS:
- Work methods, procedures, training, and supervision to reduce exposure
- Examples: Anti-bullying policies, job rotation, limiting exposure time, permit systems, warning signs, inductions
- Less reliable than Level 2 — depend on human behaviour

LEVEL 4 — PERSONAL PROTECTIVE EQUIPMENT (PPE) (least effective):
- The lowest order control measure
- Limits exposure only if worn and used correctly — every time
- Must NOT be used as the only or primary control measure if higher-order controls are practicable
- Examples: Hard hats, gloves, safety glasses, hearing protection, respirators, harnesses

COMBINATIONS: More than one control from different levels can and should be combined. A single control is rarely sufficient for high-risk hazards.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Exclusive reliance on PPE (e.g. earplugs only) for a noisy machine when a sound enclosure is practicable = hierarchy violation
- Safety signs only used as the sole barrier to a fall edge instead of a physical guardrail = non-compliant control level
- Guardrails or machine guards removed or bypassed = Level 2 control failure
- Workers relying on administrative procedure alone (e.g. "don't go near the edge") with no physical barrier = inadequate control
- PPE worn but visibly inadequate for the hazard (wrong type, damaged, poor fit) = Level 4 control failure

CHUNK 6: Step 4 — Reviewing Control Measures
Section: s5; WHS Regulation s38
Topic: Mandatory triggers for reviewing safety controls

A review of control measures IS REQUIRED when:
- The control measure is NOT EFFECTIVE in controlling the risk
- BEFORE A CHANGE at the workplace that is likely to give rise to a new or different health and safety risk
- If a NEW HAZARD OR RISK is identified
- If the results of CONSULTATION indicate a review is necessary
- If a HEALTH AND SAFETY REPRESENTATIVE (HSR) requests a review
- After an INCIDENT or near miss

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- Control measures introducing new problems (e.g. a long hose becoming a trip hazard) = review required immediately
- A scheduled review date on a risk register that has already passed = compliance failure
- Incident reports showing the same injury recurring = current controls are not effective; review mandatory
- New plant, equipment or chemicals introduced without updated risk assessment = review not conducted

CHUNK 7: Record Keeping
Section: s6
Topic: Documentation for demonstrating compliance

USEFUL RECORDS to maintain:
- Identified hazards and chosen control measures
- How and when controls were implemented and monitored
- Who was consulted
- Training records (date, topic, attendees, signatures)

MANDATORY RETENTION: For some hazards (e.g. hazardous chemicals, plant), records MUST be kept for the time specified in the WHS Regulation.

SAFE WORK METHOD STATEMENTS (SWMS): Must be available at the location of high-risk construction work — not just in the site office.

VISUAL INDICATORS:
- PCBU unable to produce a risk register during an inspection = serious documentation gap
- No training records (date, topic, signatures) for a safety procedure = record-keeping failure
- SWMS not available at the location of high-risk work = regulatory breach
- Risk register with no review dates or responsible persons = inadequate documentation
- Hazard identification forms blank or incomplete = process not being followed
